\section{The Problem with Arabic OCR}
\label{sec:intro_problem}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------------

The digitisation of Arabic text is a prerequisite for a broad class of
downstream applications: full-text search over historical archives, neural
machine translation of Arabic news and literature, text-to-speech systems
for visually impaired users, and information extraction from scanned
government documents. The technology that converts images of text into
machine-readable character sequences --- optical character recognition (OCR)
--- is the entry point for all of these applications. When OCR fails, the
failure propagates: a misread character changes word meaning, a sequence of
recognition errors degrades translation quality, and a corrupted document
image cannot be retrieved by keyword search.

Arabic presents challenges that English OCR does not face. The Arabic script
is cursive: letters connect within words and their shapes change depending on
their position in the word (isolated, initial, medial, or final). There are
no clear boundaries between characters within a word, so segmentation and
recognition are tightly coupled --- a segmentation error directly produces a
recognition error. Many Arabic letters share the same base skeleton and
differ only in the number and placement of small dots: the pair \RL{ب}/\RL{ي}
(ba/ya) differs only in whether the dot is below or above the baseline;
\RL{ف}/\RL{ق} (fa/qaf) differs only in having one or two dots above. Ink
irregularities, low scan resolution, or font variation can shift a dot from
its correct position and cause the OCR engine to output the wrong letter.

Short vowels (diacritics, or \textit{tashkeel}) are generally optional in
modern printed Arabic and are absent from most typewritten text, yet their
presence or absence changes word meaning and complicates matching between
OCR output and ground truth. Classical and modern standard Arabic coexist
with several regional dialects, each with partially distinct orthographies.
Ligatures --- special glyphs for common letter combinations --- further
increase the visual complexity that OCR engines must resolve.

The consequence of these challenges is that open-source Arabic OCR engines
produce recognisable error patterns. Character-level confusions between
visually similar letter pairs, word-level segmentation failures, and
occasional catastrophic output loops inflate the character error rate (CER)
in ways that downstream applications cannot silently absorb. A single
confused character in a word can change its meaning entirely; a repeated
output loop corrupts an entire document page.

\section{The Open-Source vs. Closed-Source Gap}
\label{sec:intro_gap}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------------

The landscape of Arabic OCR is divided between commercial closed-source
systems and open-source alternatives. Closed-source vision--language models
(VLMs) --- including GPT-4V \cite{brown2020language}, Gemini, and similar
systems --- handle Arabic document recognition with high accuracy because
they were trained on massive multimodal corpora that include Arabic text in
diverse scripts, fonts, and document layouts. These systems are not publicly
auditable, cannot be deployed in offline or sensitive environments, and charge
per-query fees that are prohibitive for large archival digitisation projects.

Open-source OCR engines such as Qaari\footnote{Qaari is an open-source
Arabic OCR system developed for modern printed Arabic text. It is the
primary OCR engine evaluated in this thesis.} offer accessibility,
reproducibility, and deployability without network dependencies. However,
they fall behind closed-source alternatives on error rate, particularly on
difficult fonts, handwritten text, and documents with low scan quality.
The gap is meaningful: a 1--2\% CER difference translates to dozens of
errors per page in a long document.

The question motivating this thesis is whether this gap can be narrowed
\emph{without changing the OCR engine itself}. Replacing an open-source
OCR engine with a closed-source one is not always feasible. But adding
a post-processing correction stage --- a language model that receives the
OCR output as text and corrects it before downstream use --- is a modular
intervention that leaves the OCR pipeline intact. If a language model can
reliably correct the systematic errors that open-source Arabic OCR engines
make, the effective accuracy of the system would improve without requiring
a new recognition model.

\section{Post-OCR Correction as a Text-to-Text Task}
\label{sec:intro_postorc}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------------

Post-OCR correction treats the OCR output as noisy text and uses a language
model to produce a denoised version. The formulation is simple: given a
sequence of characters or words output by the OCR engine, produce the most
likely corresponding sequence in the original document. This is a
text-to-text task --- no images are required after the OCR stage --- and can
be approached with any sequence-to-sequence model.

The decoupling of the vision stage (OCR) from the language stage (correction)
has several practical advantages. It is engine-agnostic: the same correction
system can be applied to output from any OCR engine. It is retroactive: it
can improve existing digitised archives without re-scanning or re-running
OCR. It is modular: improvements to the OCR engine and improvements to the
corrector can be developed independently. And it is accessible: language
models capable of Arabic text processing are available as open-source
checkpoints that can be run without proprietary APIs.

The rise of large language models (LLMs) trained on massive multilingual
corpora opens a new avenue for post-OCR correction. Unlike classical
correction approaches that require a specialised language model trained
on OCR error pairs \cite{tong1996statistical}, a general-purpose
instruction-tuned LLM can be directed to perform correction tasks through
a natural-language prompt. This \emph{prompt engineering} approach requires
no task-specific training, no labelled correction pairs, and no model
fine-tuning. The question is whether such a model --- given only a prompt
and the OCR text --- can correct Arabic OCR errors effectively, and whether
injecting additional knowledge into the prompt (error patterns, orthographic
rules, examples) can further improve performance.

\section{Research Questions}
\label{sec:intro_rq}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------------

This thesis investigates the following research questions.

\textbf{RQ1:} What are the characteristics of errors produced by Qaari on
Arabic typewritten and handwritten text? How do error profiles differ across
fonts and script types?

\textbf{RQ2:} Can a zero-shot instruction-tuned LLM correct Arabic OCR errors
without any task-specific guidance? How does correction effectiveness vary
across fonts with different baseline error rates?

\textbf{RQ3:} Does injecting OCR-specific error knowledge (character confusion
matrix) into the prompt improve correction beyond zero-shot?

\textbf{RQ4:} Does injecting explicit Arabic orthographic rules into the prompt
improve correction?

\textbf{RQ5:} Does providing error-correction examples from the QALB corpus
as few-shot demonstrations improve correction?

\textbf{RQ6:} Does morphological post-processing via CAMeL Tools --- reverting
LLM-introduced morphological invalids to the OCR original --- improve the
quality of corrected text?

\textbf{RQ7:} Does self-reflective prompting --- injecting statistical
summaries of the model's own correction failures, derived from a training
split --- improve correction on a held-out evaluation set?

\textbf{RQ8:} Can automated prompt optimisation via DSPy discover prompts
that outperform hand-crafted strategies?

\textbf{RQ9 (overarching):} Which combination of knowledge augmentation
strategies produces the best correction quality, and what does each component
individually contribute in ablation?

RQ1 is answered in Chapter~\ref{ch_results}, Section~\ref{sec:res_phase1}.
RQ2--RQ7 are each answered by a corresponding isolated experiment
(Phases~2--4D). RQ8 is answered by Phase~7 (DSPy optimisation). RQ9 is
answered by the combination and ablation study (Phase~5). Research questions
RQ2--RQ9 all take Phase~2 zero-shot correction as their reference point for
comparison.

\section{Contributions}
\label{sec:intro_contributions}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------------

This thesis makes the following contributions.

\begin{enumerate}

  \item \textbf{A systematic multi-dataset evaluation framework for Arabic
  post-OCR correction.} Seven prompt-engineering strategies are evaluated
  across nine datasets (eight PATS-A01 typewritten fonts and KHATT
  handwritten text) using aligned train/validation splits, each changing
  exactly one variable relative to the zero-shot baseline. This isolation
  design enables direct attribution of observed CER changes to individual
  knowledge sources, and the multi-font evaluation reveals font-dependent
  effects invisible in single-dataset studies.

  \item \textbf{A three-stage export--infer--analyse pipeline.} The
  experimental infrastructure separates local data preparation from GPU
  inference from result analysis. Inference is executed on Kaggle (free GPU
  quota) via a unified \texttt{scripts/infer.py} script that handles
  resumption, HuggingFace synchronisation, and prompt versioning. This
  design enables full reproducibility without a local GPU.

  \item \textbf{A novel self-reflective prompting strategy.} Phase~4D
  derives correction guidance from the model's own training-split error
  statistics --- quantifying fix rates and error introduction rates per
  error type, converting them into Arabic-language diagnostic sentences,
  and injecting them into the system prompt for validation-split inference.
  This strategy is, to our knowledge, the first application of
  self-diagnostic statistics as prompt augmentation for Arabic OCR
  correction.

  \item \textbf{An aligned multi-font evaluation design.} All eight PATS-A01
  fonts share 2,766 text lines drawn from the same underlying content, with
  an aligned 85/15 split (seed 42) ensuring identical 415-sample validation
  sets across fonts. This controlled design isolates font as the sole
  variable, enabling principled cross-font comparisons of both OCR quality
  and LLM correction effectiveness.

  \item \textbf{An empirical characterisation of the over-correction problem.}
  Zero-shot LLM correction effectiveness depends strongly on the baseline
  error rate. On low-error fonts, the LLM introduces more errors than it
  fixes; on high-error fonts, the net effect is positive. This
  font-dependent over-correction threshold is quantified across all eight
  typewritten fonts.

  \item \textbf{A comprehensive error taxonomy for Qaari across multiple
  fonts.} Phase~1 produces per-font character-level confusion matrices,
  error taxonomies, and morphological analyses, revealing how error profiles
  shift across fonts --- from dot confusion on clean fonts to more diverse
  error distributions on visually complex typefaces.

  \item \textbf{Open-source code and reproducible configuration.}
  All pipelines, prompt templates, knowledge base loaders, and evaluation
  scripts are publicly available under a permissive licence. Every
  experimental result is reproducible from the repository using the provided
  configuration files and inference notebooks.

\end{enumerate}

\section{Thesis Organisation}
\label{sec:intro_organisation}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------------

The remainder of this thesis is organised as follows.

\textbf{Chapter~\ref{ch_background} --- Background and Literature Review}
builds the necessary foundations. It covers the Arabic language and script,
the evolution of OCR from template matching through deep learning to
vision--language models, the development of large language models and
instruction tuning, prompt engineering strategies (zero-shot, few-shot,
chain-of-thought, self-reflective), and a survey of related work on
post-OCR correction and Arabic NLP. A reader familiar with any of these
topics may skip the corresponding subsection.

\textbf{Chapter~\ref{ch_system} --- System Design and Datasets}
describes the experimental infrastructure. It introduces the PATS-A01 and
KHATT datasets, explains the aligned multi-font train/validation split
design, the three-stage export--infer--analyse pipeline, defines the text
normalisation and CER/WER evaluation protocol, and documents each knowledge
base component (confusion matrix loader, rules loader, QALB loader, CAMeL
Tools integration, and LLM insights loader). The LLM correction module and
PromptBuilder class hierarchy are also described here.

\textbf{Chapter~\ref{ch_methodology} --- Experimental Methodology}
describes each experimental phase as a self-contained experiment with a
stated hypothesis, method, and expected output. Phases~1 through~4D are
covered as isolated experiments, followed by Phase~5 (combinations and
ablation) and Phase~7 (DSPy optimisation), with particular attention to the
isolation principle that ensures each augmentation condition can be attributed
to a single knowledge source.

\textbf{Chapter~\ref{ch_results} --- Results and Analysis}
reports all numerical results across the nine evaluation datasets.
Per-font Phase~1 baselines establish the OCR error landscape, Phase~2
reveals font-dependent correction effectiveness, Phases~3--4D quantify
the impact of each knowledge source, Phase~5 tests combinations and
ablation, and Phase~7 evaluates automated prompt optimisation. Every result
is stated in a table and interpreted in prose.

\textbf{Chapter~\ref{ch_conc} --- Discussion and Conclusion}
interprets the findings, develops the font-dependent over-correction
threshold as a unified theoretical argument, discusses the conditions under
which the results would likely differ, states practical system design
recommendations, enumerates limitations, and charts future research
directions. A reader primarily interested in conclusions and implications
may begin with this chapter.

The appendices provide supporting material: Appendix~A contains the full
prompt templates for all phases in both Arabic and English; Appendix~B
contains the Phase~4D Arabic-language diagnostic text generated from
training-split analysis; Appendix~C contains the relevant sections of the
configuration file; and Appendix~D contains representative tables of
OCR error examples from Phase~1 across multiple fonts.
