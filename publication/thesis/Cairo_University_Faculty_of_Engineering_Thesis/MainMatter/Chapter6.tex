\section{Revisiting the Research Questions}
\label{sec:disc_rq}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection*{RQ1: OCR Error Characterisation}

\textbf{Question:} What are the characteristics of errors produced by Qaari
on Arabic typewritten text? What error types dominate, and how severe is the
overall degradation?

\textbf{Finding:} Qaari's primary failure mode on the PATS-A01 Akhbar font
is a catastrophic repetition artefact that affects 0.5\% of samples and
accounts for 95.2\% of all character errors (CER 17.01\% overall, 1.37\% on
normal samples). On normal samples, the dominant error class is dot confusion
(\RL{Ř}/\RL{Ů}, \RL{í}/\RL{Č}, \RL{Ý}/\RL{Ţ} and related pairs), and all OCR errors produce morphologically
valid Arabic forms.

\textbf{Significance:} The high OCR quality on normal samples (CER 1.37\%)
places this dataset in the regime where post-OCR correction is most risky.
When fewer than 2\% of characters are erroneous, a corrector that cannot
reliably identify which characters are wrong will corrupt more correct
characters than it fixes. This dataset is therefore not a favourable context
for LLM-based correction, and the subsequent results must be interpreted in
that light.

\textbf{Caveat:} The Akhbar font is a well-rendered typewritten font with
unambiguous glyph shapes. Other PATS-A01 fonts or handwritten text (KHATT)
may exhibit higher error rates and different error distributions, which could
produce qualitatively different results for the correction phases.

\subsection*{RQ2: Zero-Shot Correction Capability}

\textbf{Question:} Can a zero-shot instruction-tuned LLM correct Arabic OCR
errors without any task-specific guidance?

\textbf{Finding:} Yes, partially. The zero-shot LLM reduces aggregate CER
from 17.01\% to 5.77\% (66\% relative reduction) by collapsing repetition
artefacts effectively. On normal samples, however, it increases CER from
1.37\% to 5.77\% (321\% relative increase), demonstrating systematic
over-correction.

\textbf{Significance:} This duality --- effective on catastrophic failures,
harmful on high-quality input --- suggests that zero-shot LLM correction
should be applied \emph{selectively}, not universally. It is a reliable
safety net for degenerate OCR output, but a liability for good OCR output.
The finding aligns with \textcite{kang2024llmocr} who observe that LLMs show strong
performance on heavily corrupted text but introduce errors on clean text.

\textbf{Caveat:} The zero-shot prompt used was the aggressive version (v1).
The conservative version (v2) might achieve a better balance between fixing
artefacts and preserving correct text, though the Phase~4D error introduction
rate of 25.06\% suggests the over-correction tendency is deep-seated and
unlikely to be fully resolved by prompt rewording alone.

\subsection*{RQ3: Confusion Matrix Injection}

\textbf{Question:} Does injecting OCR-specific error knowledge (confusion
matrix) into the prompt improve correction beyond zero-shot?

\textbf{Finding:} No. Confusion matrix injection increases CER by 240\%
relative to Phase~2 (19.62\% vs. 5.77\%), producing the second-worst
result overall.

\textbf{Significance:} Injecting a list of characters the model should
scrutinise increases, not decreases, its intervention rate on those characters.
This is a concrete demonstration of the prompt-as-activation-signal problem:
informing the model that certain confusions exist activates its correction
reflex on those characters, regardless of whether those characters are
actually confused in the input. The model cannot distinguish between
``the confusion matrix says this pair is confusable'' and ``this specific
character in this specific token is confused.''

\subsection*{RQ4: Rule-Augmented Prompting}

\textbf{Question:} Does injecting explicit Arabic orthographic rules improve
correction?

\textbf{Finding:} No. Rule augmentation increases CER by 91\% relative to
Phase~2 (11.03\% vs. 5.77\%).

\textbf{Significance:} Prescriptive orthographic rules apply to all text, not
just to OCR errors. The model applies them to correctly rendered tokens,
producing over-normalisation. This is consistent with the known trade-off
between generality and precision in linguistic rule injection: general rules
cover many phenomena but also fire on correct text.

\subsection*{RQ5: Few-Shot Examples}

\textbf{Question:} Does providing error-correction examples from the QALB
corpus improve correction?

\textbf{Finding:} No, but Phase~4B is the best augmented result. CER
increases by 75\% relative to Phase~2 (10.09\% vs. 5.77\%), the smallest
degradation among active augmentation conditions.

\textbf{Significance:} Example-based guidance is more effective than abstract
rules or character lists at constraining the model's behaviour. Showing
specific input--output pairs implicitly communicates the expected scale of
correction, reducing the gratuitous rewriting that characterises Phases 3, 4A,
and 5. The residual degradation reflects the QALB transfer gap: human typing
errors and OCR visual-confusion errors are different distributions, and the
QALB examples do not teach the model how to handle repetition artefacts,
dot-position swaps, or the conservative posture required for high-quality
input.

\subsection*{RQ6: CAMeL Morphological Post-Processing}

\textbf{Question:} Does morphological post-processing via CAMeL Tools improve
the quality of LLM-corrected text?

\textbf{Finding:} No, and it has zero effect (0\% CER change; zero reverts
on 200 samples).

\textbf{Significance:} The revert strategy addresses a failure mode ---
LLM-introduced morphological invalids --- that does not occur in this dataset.
When both OCR errors and LLM corrections are morphologically valid Arabic
forms (as confirmed by Phase~1 morphological analysis), the revert condition
is never satisfied. This result clarifies the scope of morphological
post-processing: it is useful only when the LLM produces non-word outputs,
which is a symptom of a poorly calibrated or insufficiently fluent model.
Qwen3-4B, as a strong multilingual model, almost never produces non-words in
Arabic.

\subsection*{RQ7: Self-Reflective Prompting}

\textbf{Question:} Does self-reflective prompting --- injecting the model's
own diagnosed failure patterns --- improve correction?

\textbf{Finding:} No. Self-reflective prompting increases CER by 82\%
relative to Phase~2 (10.48\% vs. 5.77\%).

\textbf{Significance:} The diagnostic statistics derived from training-split
analysis are accurate (97.35\% insertion fix rate, 25.06\% overall introduction
rate), but injecting them into the prompt does not translate the accurate
diagnosis into improved behaviour. The model's instruction-following training
interprets the diagnostic warnings as additional licence to intervene, not
as constraints to hold back. This finding has implications beyond OCR
correction: it suggests that self-diagnostic prompting strategies \cite{shinn2023reflexion}
may be less effective when the model's primary failure mode is
over-intervention rather than under-intervention. Reflexion-style feedback
was designed for tasks where the model is not trying hard enough; in this
setting, the model is trying too hard.

\subsection*{RQ8: Retrieval-Augmented Generation}

\textbf{Question:} Does retrieval-augmented generation grounded in the
OpenITI corpus improve correction?

\textbf{Finding:} No, and it produces the worst result overall. RAG increases
CER by 283\% relative to Phase~2 (22.10\% vs. 5.77\%), exceeding even the
all-samples OCR baseline (17.01\%).

\textbf{Significance:} Domain mismatch between the classical OpenITI corpus
and modern Akhbar newspaper text is fatal to the RAG strategy in this
setting. Retrieved passages are used by the model as stylistic templates,
importing archaic vocabulary and constructions that do not belong in the
target text. The misleadingly high embedding similarity scores (average 0.805)
illustrate a known weakness of general-purpose embedding models: they compute
similarity based on shared surface tokens (common Arabic words, the definite
article) rather than topical or semantic relevance.

\subsection*{RQ9: Best Combination (Phase 6)}

\textbf{Question:} Which combination of knowledge augmentation strategies
produces the best correction, and what does each component individually
contribute?

\textbf{Finding:} Phase~6 results are pending. Based on the isolated results,
combinations that include few-shot examples (the best isolated augmentation)
and exclude RAG (the worst) are the most likely candidates for improvement.
Whether any combination can exceed the zero-shot Phase~2 baseline remains
an open question.

\section{The Over-Correction Problem}
\label{sec:disc_overcorrection}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------------

The central unexpected finding of this study is that \emph{every active
knowledge augmentation strategy tested degrades correction quality relative
to zero-shot}, and that the shared mechanism of degradation is
\emph{over-correction}: the LLM modifies tokens that are already correct.
This section develops a unified account of the phenomenon.

\subsection{What Over-Correction Is}
\label{subsec:disc_oc_what}

Over-correction refers to the LLM changing tokens in the OCR output that
correspond to correct characters in the ground truth. On a corpus where the
normal-samples OCR CER is 1.37\%, the expected number of erroneous characters
per sentence is very low. Any intervention on a correct character introduces
a new error that did not exist before correction.

The Phase~4D training-split analysis provides a precise quantification:
Qwen3-4B-Instruct-2507 has an overall error introduction rate of 25.06\%.
This means that, on average, for every four characters that were correctly
recognised by Qaari, the LLM incorrectly modifies one of them. For specific
error categories, the introduction rates are even more severe: 163.27\% for
substitution errors (the LLM introduces 1.63 new substitution errors for
every substitution it fixes) and 212.50\% for deletion errors (the LLM
introduces 2.13 new deletion errors for every deletion it fixes). The only
category where the LLM excels is insertion errors (repetition artefacts),
where it achieves a 97.35\% fix rate with only an 18.13\% introduction rate.

The practical consequence is clear. Given that Qaari on the Akhbar font
makes very few errors on normal text, the LLM's 25.06\% introduction rate
far outweighs the small number of errors it fixes. Correction makes things
worse for the typical sample.

\subsection{Why Over-Correction Happens}
\label{subsec:disc_oc_why}

The mechanism is rooted in instruction-tuning. A language model trained to
follow the instruction ``correct this text'' has been trained on data where
the correct action is to produce a modified output. The training signal
rewards modification: responses that return the input unchanged score poorly
against a correction reward, while responses that make changes --- even
small, unnecessary ones --- are more consistent with the training distribution.

The conservative instruction appended to all prompts in this study ---
``if the text is already correct, return it unchanged'' --- partially
mitigates this tendency but cannot fully counteract it. The model has a strong
prior toward intervention, and a single sentence in the system prompt
cannot override thousands of training examples that demonstrate correction as
the expected behaviour.

This interpretation is consistent with the psychology of LLM generation: the
model samples from a conditional distribution $P(\text{output} \mid
\text{instruction}, \text{input})$ where the instruction primes it toward
corrective outputs. Arabic words that could plausibly have alternative forms
(e.g., a word with a hamza whose placement could vary) will be corrected even
when the original form is already the appropriate one.

\subsection{Why Augmentation Makes Over-Correction Worse}
\label{subsec:disc_oc_augmentation}

Every knowledge augmentation element added to the prompt signals to the model
that the input text is problematic and that active correction is warranted.
Each element acts as an \emph{activation signal} for the model's correction
behaviour:

\begin{itemize}
  \item A confusion list says: ``the OCR engine confuses these characters ---
        be suspicious of these characters in the input.'' The model responds
        by becoming suspicious of those characters and changing them, even
        when they are correct.
  \item Orthographic rules say: ``apply these rules to the text.'' The model
        applies them universally, including to correctly rendered tokens.
  \item Few-shot examples say: ``here is what correction looks like.'' The
        model attempts to produce output at a similar edit distance to the
        examples, even when the input is already correct.
  \item Self-reflective diagnostics say: ``you tend to make these errors.''
        The model interprets this as evidence that the input is likely to
        contain errors and intervenes more.
  \item Retrieved passages provide alternative text that the model implicitly
        treats as a corrective reference, pulling its output toward the
        retrieved content.
\end{itemize}

The cumulative effect of adding more augmentation elements is a monotonic
increase in the model's intervention rate, which translates directly into
a monotonic increase in CER on high-quality input. The ranking in
Table~\ref{tab:main_comparison} (Chapter~\ref{ch_results}) is consistent with
this account: Phase~4B (least activating augmentation, 75\% degradation) is
the best; Phase~5 (most foreign reference text injected, 283\% degradation) is
the worst.

\subsection{Connection to the Broader Literature}
\label{subsec:disc_oc_literature}

Over-correction in LLMs is not unique to OCR correction. Analogous effects
have been observed in related settings.

In \emph{fact verification}, language models provided with retrieved passages
sometimes introduce hallucinations that align with the retrieved content
rather than the ground truth \cite{lewis2020retrieval}. The model treats the
retrieved content as authoritative and modifies its output accordingly ---
a direct parallel to the Phase~5 RAG behaviour observed here.

In \emph{chain-of-thought prompting} \cite{wei2022chain}, models that reason
step-by-step sometimes arrive at wrong answers that they would have answered
correctly with a direct response. Extended reasoning gives the model more
opportunities to drift from the correct answer. The self-reflective prompting
in Phase~4D is analogous: the model's extended reflection on its failure
patterns gives it more opportunities to introduce unsolicited corrections.

More broadly, the finding that \emph{simpler prompts outperform augmented
prompts} on this task runs counter to the prevailing assumption in prompt
engineering research that more information is always better. It suggests that
the optimal augmentation level depends on the error density of the input:
high-error-density input benefits from more guidance; low-error-density input
is harmed by it.

\section{When Would Augmentation Help?}
\label{sec:disc_when}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------------

The findings of this study are specific to a well-defined experimental
context: a high-quality typewritten corpus, a single OCR engine, a single
4-billion-parameter LLM, and a single document domain. The results would
likely differ under the following conditions.

\paragraph{Higher baseline CER.}
If the OCR output had a baseline CER of 20--30\% (as might be expected on
difficult fonts, degraded documents, or handwritten text such as KHATT),
there would be proportionally more errors to fix and fewer correct tokens to
corrupt. The LLM's 25.06\% introduction rate would still hold, but it would
represent a smaller fraction of the total token count. In that regime,
the net effect of correction could be positive, and knowledge augmentation
that further increases the intervention rate might produce net benefit.
Evaluating on KHATT is the highest-priority next step for this reason.

\paragraph{Domain-matched retrieval corpus.}
Phase~5 is a worst-case RAG evaluation: OpenITI classical Arabic against
PATS-A01 modern Arabic newspaper text. Building a retrieval corpus from
modern Arabic news articles (or, ideally, from the same news agency as the
Akhbar font) would remove the domain mismatch and allow the retrieval to
surface genuinely relevant passages. Under these conditions, RAG could provide
contextual grounding that helps the model distinguish between alternative
word forms rather than providing misleading stylistic templates.

\paragraph{OCR-native few-shot pairs.}
Phase~4B uses QALB pairs derived from human typing errors. The transfer gap
to OCR errors is imperfect. Generating few-shot pairs by running Qaari on
clean ground-truth text, pairing the OCR output with the original, and
selecting pairs that illustrate dot-confusion corrections and repetition
collapses would provide examples that directly target the OCR error
distribution. This is straightforward to implement and is expected to
significantly improve Phase~4B outcomes.

\paragraph{Larger or fine-tuned models.}
Qwen3-4B is a relatively compact model. Larger models (Qwen3-14B, GPT-4)
may have a stronger implicit prior toward conservatism, having been exposed
to more text and more instruction-following training that includes explicit
``do not change what is correct'' examples. Alternatively, fine-tuning a
model specifically for OCR correction, with training examples that reward
returning correct text unchanged, would directly address the over-correction
tendency. Such a model would have a different intervention prior and might
benefit substantially from knowledge augmentation.

\paragraph{Confidence-based selective correction.}
Rather than applying LLM correction to all samples uniformly, a selective
strategy could route only those samples likely to contain errors to the
corrector. OCR engines often produce character-level confidence scores;
samples below a confidence threshold could be flagged for correction while
high-confidence outputs are left untouched. This would limit the LLM's
exposure to correct text and substantially reduce the over-correction rate.

\section{Implications for System Design}
\label{sec:disc_design}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------------

The following practical recommendations are derived from the experimental
findings.

\paragraph{For high-quality typewritten Arabic OCR: use zero-shot, do not augment.}
On input where the baseline CER is below 5\%, adding knowledge augmentation
to the LLM prompt is actively harmful. The zero-shot corrector should be
preferred over any augmented variant. The conservative instruction (``return
unchanged if correct'') should be included, though it provides only partial
protection against over-correction.

\paragraph{Apply LLM correction selectively, not universally.}
The LLM is reliable for correcting catastrophic OCR failures (repetition
artefacts, severe degradation) but harmful for high-quality output. A
detection step --- either using OCR confidence scores or a simple
length-ratio heuristic (LLM output significantly shorter than OCR output
signals repetition collapse) --- should precede correction, routing only
high-error samples to the LLM.

\paragraph{The LLM is a safety net for runaway artefacts.}
The 97.35\% insertion fix rate demonstrates that zero-shot LLM correction is
a highly effective remedy for the Qaari repetition bug. Deploying the LLM
specifically as an artefact detector for samples whose OCR output length
exceeds some multiple of the expected text length (e.g., 5$\times$) would
deliver the primary benefit of Phase~2 at a fraction of the inference cost.

\paragraph{Include CAMeL post-processing as a zero-cost defensive layer.}
Phase~4C has zero effect in this setting but also zero downside. It adds
negligible computational cost (a morphological lookup per token, cached with
an LRU cache). For settings with a less fluent LLM, or with more
morphologically complex error types, it could provide benefit. Its inclusion
is recommended as a defensive measure that cannot degrade results.

\paragraph{Do not deploy RAG unless the retrieval corpus is domain-matched.}
Phase~5 demonstrates that a domain-mismatched retrieval corpus is actively
harmful, producing the worst result across all conditions. The retrieval
corpus must be drawn from the same domain, register, and time period as the
target documents. Before deploying RAG in a production Arabic OCR pipeline,
retrieval precision should be validated on held-out samples and a CER
threshold should be applied to confirm the corpus is helping, not hurting.

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:disc_limitations}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------------

This study acknowledges the following limitations.

\paragraph{Single dataset.}
All quantitative results are derived from 200 samples of the PATS-A01
Akhbar font, training split. This is a single typewritten Arabic font from
a specific newspaper corpus. Results may not generalise to other PATS-A01
fonts (some of which have higher OCR error rates), to handwritten text
(KHATT), or to other document domains (historical manuscripts, printed books,
signage).

\paragraph{Single OCR engine.}
Qaari's error characteristics --- particularly the repetition artefact and
the preponderance of dot-confusion substitutions --- shape all downstream
results. An OCR engine with different failure modes (e.g., Tesseract, TrOCR,
or commercial engines) would present a different error distribution to the
LLM. The confusion matrix injection (Phase~3), for instance, might behave
differently if the confusion matrix reflected a different set of character
pairs.

\paragraph{Single LLM.}
Qwen3-4B-Instruct-2507 is the sole model evaluated. The model's size (4B
parameters), training data (multilingual instruction tuning), and inference
configuration (greedy decoding, no thinking mode) are specific choices that
may not represent the behaviour of other instruction-tuned models. Larger
models may over-correct less; Arabic-specific models may handle dot-confusion
errors differently.

\paragraph{No statistical significance testing on isolated phases.}
Phases 2--5 are each evaluated on the same 200 samples with no held-out
test set. The differences between phases (e.g., Phase~2 CER 5.77\% vs.
Phase~4B CER 10.09\%) are reported without paired statistical tests.
Phase~6 is designed to include paired t-tests and bootstrap confidence
intervals, but the isolated phase results are presented as descriptive
statistics only.

\paragraph{Phase 6 not yet executed.}
The combination and ablation study (Phase~6, answering RQ9) is designed but
not yet executed. The most important research question --- which combination
of knowledge sources produces the best result --- remains unanswered. The
discussion in Section~\ref{sec:disc_rq} (RQ9) is therefore speculative.

\paragraph{QALB transfer gap.}
Phase~4B few-shot examples are drawn from the QALB corpus, which contains
human typing errors rather than OCR visual-confusion errors. The transfer
gap between these two distributions limits the ecological validity of the
few-shot condition. The Phase~4B result should be interpreted as a lower
bound on what few-shot correction could achieve with OCR-native examples.

\paragraph{Classical corpus for RAG.}
Phase~5 uses OpenITI, a corpus of classical and historical Arabic texts.
This is a worst-case domain match for the modern Akhbar newspaper corpus.
The 283\% CER increase should not be interpreted as evidence that RAG cannot
help Arabic OCR correction in general; it should be interpreted as evidence
that a severely domain-mismatched retrieval corpus causes catastrophic
degradation.

\paragraph{Diacritic stripping in evaluation.}
All CER and WER figures are computed after stripping diacritics from both
the reference and hypothesis texts (Section~\ref{subsec:norm}).
This is the standard evaluation protocol for Arabic NLP, as diacritics are
optional in most printed Arabic. However, if the application requires
diacritics (e.g., Quranic text, children's educational material), the
evaluation protocol and the correction task would be more demanding.

\section{Future Work}
\label{sec:disc_future}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------------

\paragraph{Evaluate on KHATT and all PATS-A01 fonts.}
The highest-priority next step is expanding evaluation to the 199 KHATT
handwritten samples and all 8 PATS-A01 fonts. Handwritten text is expected
to exhibit higher baseline CER, which may reverse the over-correction
dynamic and produce positive results for knowledge augmentation. Evaluating
across all 8 fonts would also reveal whether the Akhbar results generalise
to other typewritten Arabic styles.

\paragraph{Generate OCR-native few-shot pairs for Phase 4B.}
Running Qaari on a clean ground-truth corpus, pairing OCR output with
the original text, and selecting pairs that exhibit dot-confusion corrections
and repetition collapses would produce few-shot examples that directly match
the OCR error distribution. This experiment requires no GPU inference
(Qaari is a local engine) and could substantially improve Phase~4B outcomes.

\paragraph{Build a domain-matched retrieval corpus for Phase 5.}
Replacing OpenITI with a modern Arabic newspaper corpus would remove the
classical--modern domain mismatch. A corpus assembled from publicly available
Arabic news sources (Aljazeera, BBC Arabic, Al-Ahram) in the Akhbar style
would provide semantically relevant retrieval candidates. The Phase~5
experimental infrastructure (FAISS index, retrieval pipeline) requires no
modification; only the source corpus changes.

\paragraph{Fine-tune Qwen3-4B for conservative OCR correction.}
Training a model on OCR correction pairs with explicit ``return unchanged''
examples for high-quality inputs would directly address the over-correction
problem. The training objective would reward the model for returning correct
text unchanged (and penalise unsolicited modifications), which would change
the model's intervention prior without requiring prompt engineering. Such a
fine-tuned model would be a stronger base for knowledge augmentation.

\paragraph{Test with larger models.}
Evaluating Qwen3-14B, GPT-4o, or Claude Sonnet on the same experimental
conditions would assess whether the over-correction tendency diminishes with
scale. Larger models have been exposed to more instruction-following data and
may have a stronger implicit conservatism. This experiment requires no changes
to the pipeline --- only the model identifier in the configuration file.

\paragraph{Study over-correction as a standalone research question.}
The 25.06\% error introduction rate observed in Phase~4D is a striking finding
that warrants dedicated investigation. Characterising the types of tokens most
likely to be over-corrected (frequency, morphological complexity, ambiguity
of alternative forms), and developing a calibrated refusal mechanism that
the model can apply at the token level, would be a valuable contribution to
the LLM reliability literature.

\paragraph{Confidence-guided selective correction.}
Combining OCR confidence scores with LLM correction decisions would limit the
corrector's exposure to high-confidence (likely correct) OCR output. A
selective routing system that only sends low-confidence samples or samples
above a length-ratio threshold to the LLM would deliver the primary
correction benefit while avoiding the majority of over-correction cases.

\paragraph{Extension to dialectal Arabic OCR.}
Egyptian, Gulf, and Levantine dialects present additional OCR challenges
(non-standard spelling, code-switching, absent diacritics) and limited
NLP tool support. Extending the experimental framework to dialectal Arabic
would require new ground-truth corpora and would test whether the
over-correction problem is specific to MSA or applies more broadly.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------------

This thesis set out to answer a single central question: \emph{Can a
lightweight open-source LLM correct Arabic OCR errors through prompt
engineering alone, and does adding more linguistic or retrieval-based
knowledge to the prompt improve the correction?}

The answer is nuanced. On the first part of the question: yes, a zero-shot
LLM can improve aggregate OCR quality, primarily by eliminating catastrophic
repetition artefacts that inflate CER. A 66\% aggregate CER reduction
(17.01\% to 5.77\%) is a substantial improvement on an all-samples basis.
On the second part of the question: no, knowledge augmentation does not
improve upon zero-shot correction. Every augmentation strategy tested ---
confusion matrix injection, orthographic rules, few-shot examples,
self-reflective diagnostics, and retrieval-augmented generation --- produced
higher CER than the zero-shot baseline, with degradations ranging from 75\%
to 283\%.

The underlying explanation is the \emph{over-correction problem}: on
high-quality typewritten Arabic text where the OCR error rate is 1.37\%,
the LLM's 25.06\% error introduction rate means that correction is net
harmful for the average sample. Adding knowledge elements to the prompt
increases the LLM's intervention rate, which worsens an already unfavourable
error-introduction-to-error-fix ratio.

The practical implication is direct: for high-quality typewritten Arabic OCR,
the simplest strategy is the best. Zero-shot correction should be applied
selectively --- only to samples suspected of containing errors, identified by
confidence scores or output-length anomalies --- and should not be augmented
with additional knowledge that signals to the model that the text is
unreliable.

The broader significance of this result extends beyond Arabic OCR. Negative
results in prompt engineering research are informative precisely because they
identify failure modes that successful systems must guard against. The finding
that more information in the prompt is not always better --- and that on
high-quality input, it is consistently worse --- is a contribution to the
collective understanding of how instruction-tuned LLMs behave when tasked
with selective modification. As LLMs are deployed in increasingly diverse
text-processing pipelines, understanding the conditions under which they
over-intervene is as important as understanding the conditions under which
they succeed.

The study of Arabic post-OCR correction is not finished. The KHATT
handwritten dataset, larger models, domain-matched retrieval corpora, and
OCR-native few-shot pairs all represent directions where the findings of
this thesis would likely look different. Future work that pursues these
directions will build on the experimental framework, the three-stage
pipeline, and the negative results documented here to develop a more complete
picture of when and how LLMs can be trusted to improve imperfect Arabic text.
