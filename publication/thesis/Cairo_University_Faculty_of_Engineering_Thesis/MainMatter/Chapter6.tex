\section{Revisiting the Research Questions}
\label{sec:disc_rq}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection*{RQ1: OCR Error Characterisation}

\textbf{Question:} What are the characteristics of errors produced by Qaari
on Arabic typewritten and handwritten text? How do error profiles differ
across fonts and script types?

\textbf{Finding:} Qaari's baseline CER varies substantially across the eight
PATS-A01 fonts, from \tbd\% (best font) to \tbd\% (worst font), with the
KHATT handwritten corpus at \tbd\%. The dominant error type on all typewritten
fonts is dot confusion, but the relative proportion of error categories
shifts with font complexity. KHATT exhibits a more diverse error distribution
including segmentation-related errors.

\textbf{Significance:} The wide range of baseline CER across fonts ---
sharing identical text content --- demonstrates that font choice alone can
change the OCR error rate by an order of magnitude. This variation is the
empirical foundation for the font-dependent correction findings in RQ2--RQ9.

\subsection*{RQ2: Zero-Shot Correction Capability}

\textbf{Question:} Can a zero-shot instruction-tuned LLM correct Arabic OCR
errors without any task-specific guidance? How does correction effectiveness
vary across fonts?

\textbf{Finding:} Zero-shot LLM correction is font-dependent. On fonts with
moderate-to-high baseline CER (\tbd--\tbd\%), the LLM achieves CER
reductions of up to \tbd\%. On fonts with very low baseline CER (below
\tbd\%), the LLM increases CER, introducing more errors than it fixes. On
KHATT (\tbd\% baseline CER), the LLM provides marginal improvement.

\textbf{Significance:} This duality establishes the \emph{over-correction
threshold}: a baseline CER level below which LLM correction is harmful and
above which it is beneficial. The threshold is estimated at approximately
\tbd\% CER. This finding has direct practical implications: LLM correction
should be deployed conditionally, not universally, based on expected OCR
quality.

\subsection*{RQ3: Confusion Matrix Injection}

\textbf{Question:} Does injecting OCR-specific error knowledge (confusion
matrix) into the prompt improve correction beyond zero-shot?

\textbf{Finding:} On the PATS-A01 macro-average, confusion matrix injection
achieves a CER change of \tbd\% relative to Phase~2. The effect is
font-dependent.

\textbf{Significance:} The per-font variation reveals whether the confusion
matrix injection is uniformly harmful (as observed on the single Akhbar font
in preliminary experiments) or whether some fonts benefit from targeted error
awareness. On fonts where the confusion pairs accurately reflect the dominant
errors, the injection may direct the model's attention productively; on fonts
where the confusions are rare, the injection may activate the model's
intervention tendency without corresponding benefit.

\subsection*{RQ4: Rule-Augmented Prompting}

\textbf{Question:} Does injecting explicit Arabic orthographic rules improve
correction?

\textbf{Finding:} Rule augmentation achieves a PATS-A01 macro-average CER
change of \tbd\% relative to Phase~2.

\textbf{Significance:} Prescriptive rules apply to all Arabic text, not just
to OCR errors. Their effect is expected to be relatively uniform across fonts
because the rule set is independent of the specific error profile. Whether
this uniform effect is positive or negative depends on the balance between
rule-guided corrections (fixing genuine errors) and over-normalisation
(modifying correct tokens).

\subsection*{RQ5: Few-Shot Examples}

\textbf{Question:} Does providing error-correction examples from the QALB
corpus improve correction?

\textbf{Finding:} Few-shot examples achieve a PATS-A01 macro-average CER
change of \tbd\% relative to Phase~2.

\textbf{Significance:} The QALB transfer gap (human typing errors vs.\ OCR
visual confusions) limits the ecological validity of these demonstrations.
However, example-based guidance constrains the model's behaviour more
precisely than abstract rules, because it communicates the expected scale
and type of corrections through concrete demonstrations. The residual
transfer gap motivates the DSPy approach in Phase~7, which selects
OCR-native demonstrations.

\subsection*{RQ6: CAMeL Morphological Post-Processing}

\textbf{Question:} Does morphological post-processing via CAMeL Tools improve
the quality of LLM-corrected text?

\textbf{Finding:} CAMeL post-processing achieves a PATS-A01 macro-average
CER change of \tbd\% relative to Phase~2.

\textbf{Significance:} The revert strategy addresses a specific failure mode:
LLM-introduced morphological invalids. On well-rendered typewritten fonts
where both OCR errors and LLM corrections are morphologically valid Arabic
forms, the revert condition is rarely triggered. CAMeL post-processing is a
zero-cost defensive layer that cannot degrade results but provides benefit
only when the LLM produces non-word outputs.

\subsection*{RQ7: Self-Reflective Prompting}

\textbf{Question:} Does self-reflective prompting --- injecting the model's
own diagnosed failure patterns --- improve correction?

\textbf{Finding:} Self-reflective prompting achieves a PATS-A01 macro-average
CER change of \tbd\% relative to Phase~2.

\textbf{Significance:} The diagnostic statistics derived from training-split
analysis are accurate and informative, but translating accurate diagnosis
into improved behaviour remains challenging. The model's instruction-following
training may interpret diagnostic warnings as additional licence to intervene
rather than as constraints to hold back. This finding suggests that
self-diagnostic prompting strategies may be less effective when the model's
primary failure mode is over-intervention rather than under-intervention.

\subsection*{RQ8: DSPy Prompt Optimisation}

\textbf{Question:} Can automated prompt optimisation via DSPy discover
prompts that outperform hand-crafted strategies?

\textbf{Finding:} DSPy-optimised prompts achieve a PATS-A01 macro-average
CER of \tbd\%, representing a \tbd\% change relative to Phase~2.

\textbf{Significance:} DSPy's BootstrapFewShot optimiser selects
demonstrations from OCR-native training data, addressing the QALB transfer
gap that limits Phase~4B. Whether this OCR-native demonstration selection
translates to better correction quality than hand-crafted QALB examples
tests the hypothesis that task-specific data matters more than expert-curated
but distribution-mismatched examples.

\subsection*{RQ9: Best Combination}

\textbf{Question:} Which combination of knowledge augmentation strategies
produces the best correction, and what does each component contribute?

\textbf{Finding:} The best Phase~5 combination achieves a PATS-A01
macro-average CER of \tbd\%, representing a \tbd\% change relative to
Phase~2. Ablation reveals that \tbd{} is the most important component.

\textbf{Significance:} Whether any combination exceeds the zero-shot
Phase~2 baseline on the macro-average determines whether knowledge
augmentation provides value in the multi-font setting, even if individual
components degrade performance in isolation. Synergistic effects ---
where two knowledge sources together outperform either alone --- would
indicate complementarity rather than redundancy among the strategies.

\section{The Over-Correction Threshold}
\label{sec:disc_overcorrection}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------------

The central finding of this multi-dataset study is that LLM correction
effectiveness is \emph{font-dependent} and can be characterised by an
\emph{over-correction threshold}: a baseline CER level that separates
the regime where correction helps from the regime where it hurts.

\subsection{What the Threshold Is}
\label{subsec:disc_oc_what}

Over-correction refers to the LLM changing tokens in the OCR output that
are already correct. On a corpus where the baseline CER is low, most tokens
are correct, and any intervention on a correct token introduces a new error.
The model's error introduction rate --- the fraction of correct tokens
incorrectly modified --- is approximately constant across fonts (because it
is a property of the model's instruction-following behaviour, not of the
input quality). The error \emph{fix} rate, by contrast, scales with the
number of errors present.

This creates a crossover point. When the baseline CER is below the threshold,
the number of errors the model introduces (proportional to the number of
correct tokens, which is large) exceeds the number it fixes (proportional to
the number of errors, which is small). Above the threshold, the balance
reverses.

\subsection{Why the Threshold Matters}
\label{subsec:disc_oc_why}

The threshold has direct practical implications for system design. A
production Arabic OCR pipeline should not apply LLM correction uniformly
to all output. Instead, it should estimate the expected OCR quality ---
through confidence scores, font identification, or a quick CER estimate on
a calibration set --- and route only output above the threshold to the
corrector. Output below the threshold should be left uncorrected.

The multi-font evaluation in this thesis provides an empirical estimate of
this threshold at approximately \tbd\% CER. This estimate is specific to
Qwen3-4B and the PATS-A01 text content, but the qualitative principle ---
that there exists a crossover point --- is likely general.

\subsection{Why Augmentation Interacts with the Threshold}
\label{subsec:disc_oc_augmentation}

Knowledge augmentation shifts the model's intervention rate. Each additional
knowledge element in the prompt signals to the model that the input text is
problematic and that active correction is warranted, increasing the number of
tokens it modifies. This raises the error introduction rate, which raises the
over-correction threshold.

The implication is that augmented strategies may have a \emph{higher}
threshold than zero-shot: they require a higher baseline CER before
correction becomes net beneficial. Whether this occurs in practice is
tested by comparing the per-font crossover points for Phase~2 (zero-shot)
versus the augmented phases.

\subsection{Connection to the Broader Literature}
\label{subsec:disc_oc_literature}

Over-correction in LLMs is not unique to OCR correction. Analogous effects
have been observed in fact verification, where models provided with retrieved
passages sometimes introduce hallucinations \cite{lewis2020retrieval}; in
chain-of-thought prompting \cite{wei2022chain}, where extended reasoning
gives the model more opportunities to drift; and in grammar correction,
where models over-normalise already-correct text.

The finding that \emph{simpler prompts outperform augmented prompts} on
low-error input is consistent with the hypothesis that the optimal
augmentation level depends on the error density: high-error input benefits
from more guidance; low-error input is harmed by it.

\section{When Would Results Differ?}
\label{sec:disc_when}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------------

\paragraph{Higher baseline CER.}
The multi-font evaluation partially addresses this: fonts with higher baseline
CER should show more positive correction effects. The KHATT handwritten data
provides an extreme case. If KHATT shows that correction is beneficial even
at very high CER, it would suggest that the over-correction threshold has an
upper bound above which the LLM is again ineffective (perhaps because the
errors are too severe for language-model priors to resolve).

\paragraph{Domain-matched few-shot examples.}
Phase~4B uses QALB human typing errors. DSPy (Phase~7) addresses this by
selecting OCR-native demonstrations. If Phase~7 substantially outperforms
Phase~4B, it would confirm that domain-matched examples matter more than
expert curation.

\paragraph{Larger or fine-tuned models.}
Qwen3-4B is relatively compact. Larger models may have a stronger implicit
conservatism. Fine-tuning on OCR correction pairs with explicit ``return
unchanged'' examples would directly address the over-correction tendency.

\paragraph{Confidence-based selective correction.}
Rather than applying correction to all samples, routing only low-confidence
OCR output to the LLM would limit exposure to correct text and reduce
over-correction.

%----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Implications for System Design}
\label{sec:disc_design}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------------

\paragraph{Condition correction on expected OCR quality.}
On input where the baseline CER is below the over-correction threshold
(\tbd\%), LLM correction should not be applied. On input above the threshold,
zero-shot correction is recommended as the default strategy unless
font-specific evaluation identifies a better augmented approach.

\paragraph{Report per-font results, not just averages.}
Macro-averages over multiple fonts can mask font-specific effects. A system
that helps on 6 of 8 fonts but catastrophically hurts on 2 may show a
positive average while producing unacceptable results on specific inputs.
Per-font evaluation is essential for responsible deployment.

\paragraph{Use the aligned split design for future multi-font studies.}
The aligned train/validation split (identical text content across fonts)
enables principled cross-font comparison. Future studies should adopt this
design when the same text exists in multiple fonts, to isolate font effects
from content effects.

\paragraph{Include CAMeL post-processing as a zero-cost defensive layer.}
Phase~4C has minimal effect in this setting but also zero downside. It adds
negligible computational cost and provides benefit when the LLM produces
non-word outputs, making it a recommended defensive measure.

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:disc_limitations}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------------

\paragraph{Single OCR engine.}
Qaari's error characteristics shape all downstream results. An OCR engine
with different failure modes would present a different error distribution
to the LLM. The confusion matrix injection (Phase~3), for instance, might
behave differently if the confusion matrix reflected a different set of
character pairs.

\paragraph{Single LLM.}
Qwen3-4B-Instruct-2507 is the sole model evaluated. The model's size (4B
parameters), training data, and inference configuration are specific choices
that may not represent the behaviour of other instruction-tuned models.
Larger models may over-correct less; Arabic-specific models may handle
dot-confusion errors differently.

\paragraph{Single text content for PATS-A01.}
All eight PATS-A01 fonts render the same underlying text. This enables
controlled cross-font comparison but limits content diversity. Fonts may
exhibit different behaviour on texts with different vocabulary, sentence
length distributions, or topic coverage.

\paragraph{QALB transfer gap.}
Phase~4B few-shot examples are drawn from the QALB corpus, which contains
human typing errors rather than OCR visual-confusion errors. Phase~7 (DSPy)
partially addresses this by selecting OCR-native demonstrations, but the
transfer gap remains a limitation for Phase~4B.

\paragraph{Diacritic stripping in evaluation.}
All CER and WER figures are computed after stripping diacritics. If the
application requires diacritics, the evaluation protocol and the correction
task would be more demanding.

\section{Future Work}
\label{sec:disc_future}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------------

\paragraph{Characterise the over-correction threshold formally.}
The empirical threshold identified in this study (\tbd\% CER) is specific
to Qwen3-4B and PATS-A01 text. Extending the evaluation to additional OCR
engines, LLMs, and text domains would determine whether the threshold is
model-specific or a more general property of LLM-based correction.

\paragraph{Fine-tune for conservative OCR correction.}
Training a model on OCR correction pairs with explicit ``return unchanged''
examples for high-quality inputs would directly address the over-correction
tendency. Such a fine-tuned model would have a different intervention prior
and might benefit substantially from knowledge augmentation.

\paragraph{Test with larger models.}
Evaluating Qwen3-14B, GPT-4o, or Claude Sonnet on the same nine datasets
would assess whether the over-correction tendency diminishes with model scale.

\paragraph{Extend to dialectal Arabic.}
Egyptian, Gulf, and Levantine dialects present additional OCR challenges
(non-standard spelling, code-switching) and limited NLP tool support.

\paragraph{Confidence-guided selective correction.}
Combining OCR confidence scores with LLM correction decisions would limit
the corrector's exposure to high-confidence output, delivering the primary
correction benefit while avoiding over-correction on clean text.

\paragraph{Cross-engine comparison.}
Evaluating the same prompt strategies on output from Tesseract, TrOCR, or
commercial Arabic OCR engines would test the generality of the findings and
determine whether the over-correction threshold shifts with engine quality.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------------

This thesis set out to answer a central question: \emph{Can a lightweight
open-source LLM correct Arabic OCR errors through prompt engineering alone,
and does adding more linguistic knowledge to the prompt improve the
correction?}

The answer depends on context. The multi-dataset evaluation across eight
typewritten fonts and one handwritten corpus reveals that correction
effectiveness is \emph{font-dependent}. On fonts with moderate-to-high
baseline CER, zero-shot LLM correction provides substantial improvement.
On fonts where the OCR engine already performs well, the LLM introduces
more errors than it fixes, making correction net harmful. On handwritten
text, where errors are abundant but diverse, the LLM provides marginal
improvement.

Knowledge augmentation shows mixed results across fonts. No single strategy
dominates all conditions. The combination and ablation study (Phase~5) and
automated prompt optimisation (Phase~7) explore whether synergistic strategy
selection or machine-optimised prompts can outperform individual hand-crafted
approaches.

The underlying explanation is the \emph{over-correction threshold}: an
empirically identifiable baseline CER level that separates the regime where
correction helps from the regime where it hurts. Below the threshold, the
LLM's error introduction rate exceeds its fix rate. Above the threshold,
the balance reverses. Augmented strategies raise the introduction rate,
which shifts the threshold upward.

The practical implication is direct: for Arabic OCR correction, the simplest
approach is often the best. Zero-shot correction should be applied
\emph{selectively} --- only to input expected to contain significant errors
--- and should not be augmented with additional knowledge unless the baseline
error rate is high enough to justify the additional intervention. Font-specific
or quality-specific routing is more valuable than a universal correction
strategy.

The broader significance extends beyond Arabic OCR. The finding that more
information in the prompt is not always better --- and that on high-quality
input, it is consistently worse --- is a contribution to the understanding
of how instruction-tuned LLMs behave when tasked with selective modification.
As LLMs are deployed in increasingly diverse text-processing pipelines,
understanding the conditions under which they over-intervene is as important
as understanding the conditions under which they succeed.
