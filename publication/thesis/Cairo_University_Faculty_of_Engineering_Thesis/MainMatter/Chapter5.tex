\subsection{Aggregate Error Rates}
\label{subsec:res_p1_aggregate}

Table~\ref{tab:p1_metrics} reports the CER and WER computed over the
200-sample PATS-A01 Akhbar training evaluation. Two views are given: the
\emph{all-samples} view includes every sample in the 200-sample set;
the \emph{normal-samples} view excludes the single runaway sample.

\begin{table}[htbp]

  \caption{Phase~1 OCR baseline metrics on PATS-A01 Akhbar (200 samples).}
  \label{tab:p1_metrics}
  \begin{tabularx}{\textwidth}{lXXXXX}
    \toprule
    \textbf{Sample Set} & \textbf{$n$} & \textbf{CER} & \textbf{WER}
      & \textbf{CER Std} & \textbf{CER Median} \\

    Normal samples   & 199 & 1.37\%  & 5.04\%  & 0.02 & 0.00\% \\

The striking discrepancy between the two rows --- 17.01\% versus 1.37\% CER
--- is caused by a single sample that exhibits Qaari's \emph{repetition
artefact}: the OCR engine enters an infinite repetition loop on a particular
text segment, generating thousands of copies of a short phrase. That one
sample (0.5\% of the set) contributes 1,964 of the 2,063 total character
errors. Including it maintains \emph{ecological validity}: the repetition bug
is real-world behaviour of the engine and appears in any large-scale
deployment. All subsequent phases are therefore evaluated on the full
200-sample set so that the ability of the LLM to collapse these repetitions
is captured.

The all-samples CER standard deviation of 2.21 and a median of 0.00\% reveal
a heavily skewed distribution. Over half of the 200 samples have zero OCR
errors; the mean is pulled upward by the single runaway and by a small number
of difficult samples. On normal samples the picture is very different: the
standard deviation drops to 0.02 and the CER of 1.37\% reflects the genuine
character-level accuracy of Qaari on clean typewritten Akhbar text.

Insertions dominate at 95.2\% because they are overwhelmingly produced by the

engine on typewritten Arabic. Deletions (1.55\% overall) represent characters

cluster in closely spaced character groups where segmentation fails.

The absence of hamza and taa marbuta errors is notable. These phenomena
account for a significant fraction of human spelling errors in Arabic but are
well-handled by Qaari's recognition model on the Akhbar font, which has
unambiguous glyph renderings for these characters.

The dominant confusion, \RL{堩~$\to$~\RL{旐, accounts for 8 of the 61
total substitutions (probability 0.89). Both characters are emphatic
consonants sharing a similar skeletal shape; the only distinguishing feature
is a small diacritic-like mark that can be rendered ambiguously at low
resolution. The bidirectional ba/ya confusion (\RL{瞷~$\leftrightarrow$~\RL{刂)
reflects the identical base shapes of these two letters and their reliance on
dot placement --- below the baseline for \RL{刂 and above the baseline for
\RL{瞷; a slight ink irregularity shifts the engine's decision. The alef
maqsura confusion (\RL{魺~$\to$~\RL{瞷) is structurally identical to the
classical NLP challenge described in Section~\ref{subsec:arabic_script}: both
characters are written without dots in many fonts and differ only in their
Unicode code point.

The fen/qaf confusion (\RL{搠~$\to$~\RL{煐) illustrates the dot-count
ambiguity: \RL{搠 has one dot above, \RL{煐 has two dots above; the
difference is a single ink dot. Collectively, dot-related confusions
(\RL{堩/\RL{旐, \RL{瞷/\RL{刂, \RL{魺/\RL{瞷, \RL{搠/\RL{煐, \RL{鸞/\RL{芑)
account for 18 of the 61 substitutions (29.5\%), confirming that dot
ambiguity is the primary substitution driver in the Akhbar font.

\subsection{Morphological Validity Analysis}
\label{subsec:res_p1_morph}

CAMeL Tools MSA morphological analysis was applied to every token in the
200-sample set. The results are unambiguous: \emph{100\% of OCR errors are
valid-but-wrong}. The confusion matrix shows zero non-word errors --- every
substituted or deleted character produces a token that is itself a valid Arabic
word, merely the wrong one. The OCR word validity rate is 100\%: all 2,801
OCR tokens are morphologically valid Arabic forms.

This finding has a profound implication for Phase~4C (morphological
post-processing). A revert strategy that identifies LLM-introduced
morphological invalids and reverts them to the (valid) OCR original cannot
operate in this regime: the OCR errors are already valid words, so simple
dictionary lookup or morphological validity checks provide no discriminative
signal. Correcting valid-but-wrong substitutions requires context, not just
lexical validity.

\textbf{Answer to RQ1:} Qaari produces systematic errors on the Akhbar
typewritten corpus. The dominant failure mode is a catastrophic repetition
artefact (0.5\% of samples, responsible for 95.2\% of all character errors)
which inflates aggregate CER to 17.01\%. On the remaining 199 normal samples,
the CER is 1.37\%, indicating high-quality recognition on clean typewritten
text. The dominant substitution error type is dot confusion (29.5\% of
substitutions), driven by near-identical letter shapes. All OCR errors produce
morphologically valid Arabic forms, precluding detection by dictionary lookup.

\subsection{Comparison to Phase 1}
\label{subsec:res_p2_vs_p1}

The transition from Phase~1 to Phase~2 must be interpreted from two different
reference points.

\paragraph{Relative to all-samples OCR (CER 17.01\%).}
The LLM achieves a 66\% relative CER reduction, falling from 17.01\% to
5.77\%. This improvement is substantially driven by the repetition artefact
sample. The LLM collapses the thousands of repeated characters into a
coherent corrected text, drastically reducing the edit distance to the ground
truth. This is the expected strength of a language model: it recognises the
output as degenerate and reconstructs the probable source sentence. The Phase
4D training-split analysis (Section~\ref{subsec:res_p4d}) later quantifies
this precisely: the LLM fixes 97.35\% of insertion errors, corroborating its
near-perfect handling of the repetition artefact.

\paragraph{Relative to normal-samples OCR (CER 1.37\%).}
The LLM \emph{increases} CER from 1.37\% to 5.77\% on the 199 normal
samples --- a 321\% relative increase. This is the \emph{over-correction
problem} stated numerically. On text that Qaari rendered correctly, the LLM
introduces errors at a rate that exceeds the errors it fixes. The model
changes tokens that are already correct, and each unsolicited change has a
high probability of being wrong (since the token was already right). This
finding recurs throughout Chapters~5 and~6 as the central obstacle to
knowledge-augmented correction on high-quality typewritten input.

\subsection{Per-Sample Analysis}
\label{subsec:res_p2_per_sample}

The Phase~2 CER standard deviation (0.12) is 21 times the mean (0.058),
indicating a highly skewed distribution. The CER median is 3.28\%, lower than
the mean, confirming that the majority of samples cluster at low CER while a
tail of high-CER samples pulls the mean upward. The 95th-percentile CER of
19.57\% shows that the worst 10 samples (5\%) incur substantial error rates,
likely including the repetition sample and cases where the LLM rewrote
extensive portions of correct text.

The shift in the median from 0.00\% (Phase~1 all-samples) to 3.28\%
(Phase~2) reveals the over-correction problem at the sample level: the modal
sample was error-free in the OCR output but is no longer error-free after LLM
correction. Phase~2 produces a worse median outcome than the original OCR on
normal samples, even though it produces a better aggregate CER by eliminating
the catastrophic repetition artefact.

\textbf{Answer to RQ2:} A zero-shot instruction-tuned LLM can substantially
reduce aggregate CER (66\% relative reduction) by collapsing OCR repetition
artefacts. However, on high-quality typewritten input where the baseline CER
is 1.37\%, the LLM introduces more errors than it fixes, raising the CER to
5.77\% on normal samples. This over-correction effect defines the ceiling for
all subsequent augmentation phases: no augmentation strategy can succeed if
the LLM is already introducing more errors than exist in the input.

The central finding is clear: \emph{no knowledge augmentation strategy

The magnitudes range from +75\% (Phase~4B, the best augmented result) to

condition is never triggered. Each phase's result is now analysed in detail.

\subsection{Phase 3 --- Confusion Matrix Injection}
\label{subsec:res_p3}

Phase~3 achieves a CER of 19.62\% and WER of 24.35\%, representing a 240\%
relative CER increase over Phase~2. This is the second-worst outcome across
all augmentation conditions.

The confusion list injected into the prompt contains the top-10 character
confusion pairs derived from the Phase~1 training data (Section~\ref{subsec:kb_confusion}).
On a corpus where dot confusions account for only 0.68\% of all errors and
29.5\% of substitutions (Table~\ref{tab:p1_taxonomy}), this injection creates
a signal-to-noise problem. The model receives a list of characters it should
scrutinise --- \RL{堩/\RL{旐, \RL{瞷/\RL{刂, and so on --- and interprets
this as evidence that the text is broadly unreliable in all dot-group
positions. In response, it substitutes characters at dot-ambiguous positions
that it would otherwise leave untouched.

The CER standard deviation of 2.19 (compared to 0.12 for Phase~2) confirms
that the confusion injection does not uniformly degrade all samples. Most
samples remain similar to Phase~2, but a small fraction of samples are
catastrophically rewritten. This bimodal effect suggests the model latches
onto the confusion information selectively: when it encounters an ambiguous
passage where multiple characters match the warned pairs, it aggressively
substitutes; when the passage is unambiguous, the confusion list has no
effect. The Phase~1 CER standard deviation of 2.21 is almost identical to
Phase~3's 2.19 --- the confusion injection has restored the same runaway-like
variance that the repetition artefact caused in Phase~1, but through LLM
over-correction rather than OCR failure.

\textbf{Answer to RQ3:} Injecting OCR-specific confusion information does not
improve correction and produces the second-largest degradation (+240\% CER
relative to Phase~2). The confusion list amplifies over-correction on
dot-ambiguous characters rather than directing the model's attention
productively.

\subsection{Phase 4A --- Rule-Augmented Prompting}
\label{subsec:res_p4a}

Phase~4A achieves a CER of 11.03\% and WER of 17.70\%, representing a 91\%
relative CER increase over Phase~2. This is the third-worst outcome.

The Arabic orthographic rules injected into the prompt (Section~\ref{subsec:kb_rules})
cover phenomena that occur broadly throughout Arabic text: hamza al-qat',
hamza al-wasl, taa marbuta, alef maqsura, and related forms. Unlike the
confusion matrix (which targets a narrow set of substitution pairs), the
rules are \emph{prescriptive for all text}. The model applies them even to
tokens that were already correct, over-normalising forms that are contextually
appropriate. For example, a rule about the appropriate form of hamza may cause
the model to rewrite a correctly rendered hamza form because the rule's surface
specification matches a token that is correct in context.

The CER standard deviation of 0.19 (higher than Phase~2's 0.12 but far below
Phase~3's 2.19) indicates more uniform degradation: the rules affect all
samples rather than selectively catastrophising a few. This is consistent with
the nature of the augmentation: prescriptive rules apply broadly, whereas
confusion pairs apply selectively.

The median CER rises from 3.28\% (Phase~2) to 6.40\% (Phase~4A), confirming
that the modal sample is worse, not just the tail. The typical Akhbar sentence
contains characters subject to the orthographic rules, so most samples incur
additional errors.

\textbf{Answer to RQ4:} Explicit Arabic orthographic rules do not improve
correction (+91\% CER relative to Phase~2). Their broad applicability causes
the model to over-normalise correct tokens at a higher rate than it corrects
genuine OCR errors.

\subsection{Phase 4B --- Few-Shot Examples}
\label{subsec:res_p4b}

Phase~4B achieves a CER of 10.09\% and WER of 15.64\%, representing a 75\%
relative CER increase over Phase~2. This is the \emph{best result among all
augmentation conditions}, though it still degrades relative to the zero-shot
baseline.

The few-shot examples from the QALB corpus constrain the model's behaviour
more precisely than abstract rules or character lists, because they demonstrate
specific input--output transformations rather than general principles. The model
can infer from the examples what magnitude of change is expected (minor lexical
corrections, not full rewrites), which partially moderates the over-correction
tendency. The CER standard deviation (0.18) and median (6.45\%) are comparable
to Phase~4A, but the aggregate CER is lower because the example-based
constraint is more effective at preventing gratuitous rewrites.

The QALB transfer gap discussed in Section~\ref{subsec:phase4b_transfer} is visible
in the residual degradation. QALB examples are drawn from human typing errors,
which follow a phonological-spelling distribution. OCR errors follow a
visual-confusion distribution. The few-shot examples do not teach the model
specifically to collapse repetition artefacts or to avoid dot-position changes;
they teach it to correct the kinds of errors humans make when typing, which
partially overlaps with but does not match the OCR error distribution.

\textbf{Answer to RQ5:} Few-shot examples from QALB improve correction
relative to other augmentation conditions but still degrade performance by
75\% CER relative to Phase~2. The transfer gap between QALB human typing
errors and Qaari OCR visual-confusion errors limits the benefit of the
QALB-derived demonstrations.

\subsection{Phase 4C --- CAMeL Morphological Post-Processing}
\label{subsec:res_p4c}

Phase~4C achieves a CER of 5.77\% and WER of 10.60\% --- \emph{identical to
Phase~2 in every metric}, including CER standard deviation (0.12) and median
(3.28\%). The revert strategy (Section~\ref{subsec:phase4c_rationale}) was triggered
zero times across all 200 samples.

The zero-revert outcome follows directly from the morphological analysis of
Phase~1 (Section~\ref{subsec:res_p1_morph}): 100\% of OCR errors are
valid-but-wrong substitutions. The LLM's corrections are also morphologically
valid Arabic forms, because Qwen3 is a fluent Arabic language model that
rarely produces non-words. The revert condition --- LLM word invalid AND OCR
word valid --- is therefore never satisfied.

Two interpretations of this result are possible. The first is that the CAMeL
revert strategy is simply unnecessary for high-quality typewritten OCR: when
the OCR errors are all valid words and the LLM corrections are all valid words,
morphological validity provides no discriminative signal. The second
interpretation is that Phase~4C's revert strategy addresses the wrong failure
mode: it is designed to catch LLM hallucinations that produce non-word outputs,
but the LLM on this dataset hallucinates \emph{valid} alternative words, not
non-words. Context-aware correction (requiring a language-modelling component,
not just a morphological oracle) would be needed to address this class of error.

The practical implication for Phase~6 is that adding CAMeL post-processing to
any combination system is unlikely to produce improvement unless the combined
prompt elicits a higher rate of morphological invalids from the LLM.

\textbf{Answer to RQ6:} CAMeL Tools morphological post-processing has no
effect on correction quality in this experimental setting (0\% CER change
relative to Phase~2, zero reverts). OCR errors and LLM corrections are both
morphologically valid Arabic forms; the revert condition is never satisfied.

\subsection{Phase 4D --- Self-Reflective Prompting}
\label{subsec:res_p4d}

Phase~4D achieves a CER of 10.48\% and WER of 16.12\%, representing an 82\%
relative CER increase over Phase~2. The training-split error analysis
(Section~\ref{sec:phase4d}) produced accurate and interpretable statistics,
reproduced here for reference:

           Akhbar training split. Fix rate: fraction of OCR errors of this
           type corrected by the LLM. Introduction rate: fraction of correct
           tokens of this type incorrectly modified by the LLM.}
  \label{tab:p4d_insights}
  \begin{tabularx}{\textwidth}{lXX}

    \bottomrule
  \end{tabularx}

The training-split analysis reveals accurate and informative statistics. The
LLM is exceptionally effective at correcting insertion errors (97.35\% fix
rate), which is consistent with the Phase~2 result for the repetition artefact.
However, it is nearly unable to correct deletion errors (3.12\% fix rate):
reconstructing a missing character requires generating content that was not in
the input, which the LLM rarely does correctly for single-character deletions
in Arabic. The substitution fix rate of 16.33\% is similarly low.

The error \emph{introduction} rates are alarming. A 25.06\% overall
introduction rate means that for every four tokens that were correct in the
OCR output, the LLM incorrectly modifies one of them. For deletion errors
(212.50\%) and other substitutions (163.27\%), the introduction rate exceeds
100\%: the LLM introduces more new errors of these types than there were in
the original OCR output.

These statistics were converted into Arabic-language diagnostic sentences and
injected into the Phase~4D prompt using \texttt{build\_self\_reflective()}.
The intent was to cause the model to apply corrections more cautiously in the
domains where it performs poorly. The result shows this does not work: the
CER increases by 82\% relative to Phase~2.

The most likely explanation is that the diagnostic information carries a
counter-productive framing. The injected text tells the model, in effect:
``You tend to introduce errors in substitution and deletion categories.'' The
model's instruction-following training interprets this as additional licence
to intervene carefully --- increasing, rather than decreasing, the number of
positions it scrutinises. Paradoxically, the diagnostic warning that says
``you over-correct'' causes the model to over-correct more, not less.

The elevated CER standard deviation (0.24) and median (6.45\%) confirm that
self-reflective prompting causes more uniform degradation across the sample
distribution than Phase~3 or Phase~5, consistent with the broad applicability
of the diagnostic warnings.

\textbf{Answer to RQ7:} Self-reflective prompting --- injecting the model's
own diagnosed failure statistics into the prompt --- does not improve
correction. The 82\% relative CER increase is consistent with the hypothesis
that diagnostic warnings activate rather than suppress the model's
intervention tendency.

\subsection{Phase 5 --- Retrieval-Augmented Generation}
\label{subsec:res_p5}

Phase~5 achieves a CER of 22.10\% and WER of 26.93\%, representing a 283\%
relative CER increase over Phase~2. This is the \emph{worst result} across
all experimental conditions and exceeds even the all-samples Phase~1 OCR
baseline (17.01\% CER).

The retrieval analysis reports an average top-1 cosine similarity of 0.805
between OCR queries and retrieved OpenITI passages. This high similarity score
is an artefact of how inner-product similarity operates on sentence embeddings:
short Arabic queries from a newspaper corpus and short passages from a
classical religious corpus share surface-level morphological and lexical
patterns (common Arabic words, definite article al-) that inflate embedding
similarity without reflecting semantic or topical relatedness. The retrieved
passages are not semantically relevant to the news sentences being corrected.

The model receives these classical Arabic passages as contextual grounding and
uses them as stylistic templates. In doing so, it imports vocabulary,
syntactic constructions, and even entire phrases from the retrieved classical
text into the correction output, replacing modern newspaper vocabulary with
archaic forms. This is the domain mismatch problem described in
Section~\ref{sec:phase5}: OpenITI classical texts and PATS-A01 Akhbar
news text are different registers of Arabic with substantially non-overlapping
lexicons.

The CER standard deviation of 1.99 (second only to Phase~3's 2.19) confirms
inconsistent, highly variable behaviour. When a retrieved passage happens to
contain vocabulary similar to the input sentence, the model's intervention is
particularly disruptive because the surface similarity masks the semantic
gap. Some samples receive almost no retrieval-driven modification (low CER),
while others are heavily rewritten (high CER).

\textbf{Answer to RQ8:} RAG grounded in the OpenITI classical Arabic corpus
does not improve correction and produces the worst result (+283\% CER relative
to Phase~2). Domain mismatch between classical OpenITI text and modern
newspaper content causes the model to import irrelevant vocabulary from
retrieved passages. High embedding similarity scores (avg. 0.805) are
misleading, reflecting surface-level lexical overlap rather than semantic
relevance.

\section{Phase 6 Results --- Combinations and Ablation}
\label{sec:res_phase6}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------------

Phase~6 tests 12 pre-selected combinations of knowledge augmentation strategies
(Section~\ref{sec:phase6}) and performs component-level ablation on the
full combined system. At the time of writing, Phase~6 inference has not yet
been executed; this section reports the experimental design and defers
quantitative results to future work.

The combinations to be evaluated are listed in Table~\ref{tab:p6_design}.
Each row identifies the combination by a short code built from the component
initials: C (confusion matrix), R (rules), E (examples/few-shot), S
(self-reflective), G (RAG/retrieval), and M (CAMeL post-processing). The
selection rationale was described in Section~\ref{sec:phase6}: pairs were
selected to combine complementary knowledge types; triples extend the

The ablation design (Section~\ref{sec:phase6}) will report CER change when
each component is removed from the full system (combination 6-12). A positive
CER change upon component removal indicates that the component contributed
positively to the combined system; a negative change indicates the component
was harmful.

Based on the isolated phase results (Table~\ref{tab:main_comparison}), several
predictions can be stated ahead of execution. First, Phase~4C (CAMeL) is
unlikely to produce improvement in any combination, since the revert condition
was never triggered in isolation and the combined system is unlikely to produce
more non-word outputs. Second, Phase~5 (RAG) is likely to remain harmful in
combination, since the domain mismatch between OpenITI and Akhbar is a
structural problem not resolved by combining with other strategies. Third,
Phase~4B (few-shot, best isolated result) is the most promising component;
combinations that include it are the most likely to approach or exceed the
Phase~2 baseline.

The ranking reveals the core finding of this study: the \emph{best result
achievable with an LLM} (Phase~2, CER 5.77\%) is still more than four times
worse than the \emph{best result achievable without an LLM} (Phase~1 normal,

accurate than the LLM corrector, and every knowledge augmentation strategy
tested makes the corrector worse than the zero-shot baseline.

The pattern of results is consistent and interpretable: every form of
additional knowledge injected into the prompt increases the model's
propensity to intervene on the input text, and on an input text that is
already 98.63\% accurate (normal-samples CER 1.37\%), intervention almost
always introduces errors. The over-correction problem, quantified at a
25.06\% error introduction rate in Phase~4D, is not a failure of any
individual augmentation strategy --- it is a property of the LLM's
instruction-following behaviour on high-quality input. Chapter~\ref{ch_conc}
analyses this finding in depth and considers the conditions under which
knowledge augmentation would produce a different outcome.
