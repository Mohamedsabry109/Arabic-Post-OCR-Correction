\setstretch{1.5}

This chapter reports the numerical results for all experimental phases
across the nine evaluation datasets (eight PATS-A01 typewritten fonts and
KHATT handwritten text). Every result is first presented in a table and
then interpreted in prose; tables serve comparison and prose serves
understanding.

Results are reported on the validation split (415 samples per PATS-A01
font; KHATT validation) with diacritics stripped before CER/WER computation.
For PATS-A01, the macro-average CER (each font contributing equally,
regardless of sample count) is reported as the primary aggregate metric;
per-font results are reported in full. Both CER and WER are presented
throughout, but CER is the primary metric used for ranking and discussion.

The eight PATS-A01 fonts exhibit substantially different baseline CER values,
spanning from 4.3\% (Akhbar) to 24.3\% (Naskh) --- a ratio of approximately
5.6:1. This variation is entirely attributable to font, since all fonts
render the same aligned text content on the validation split. Akhbar, a clean
modern typeface with well-separated dots and unambiguous glyph shapes, is
the easiest font for Qaari to recognise. Naskh, a classical font with more
complex ligature structures and greater glyph density, presents the greatest
recognition challenge. The PATS-A01 macro-average CER is 14.2\%.

KHATT handwritten text (44.6\% mean CER) presents a qualitatively different
challenge from all eight typewritten fonts. The P95 value of 82.3\% indicates
that the worst-performing 5\% of KHATT samples have near-total recognition
failure, a condition not observed on any typewritten font.

Within each dataset, the CER median is substantially lower than the mean,
and the P95 is substantially higher. This right-skewed distribution
indicates that a minority of samples account for a disproportionate share of
total errors. On Akhbar, for example, the median CER is 2.8\% while the
mean is 4.3\% and the P95 is 14.2\%, suggesting that most samples are
recognised accurately but a small number contain severe errors (likely
corresponding to specific challenging content rather than font-wide issues).

On PATS-A01, 42.1\% of all errors are dot confusions and 18.3\% are
similar-shape substitutions --- together accounting for over 60\% of all
errors. These are precisely the visual confusion types that a character
confusion matrix can capture and that an informed LLM prompt can potentially
target. By contrast, on KHATT, segmentation errors (word merges at 19.6\%,
word splits at 17.3\%) constitute nearly 37\% of all errors, a category
that is largely absent from typewritten text. Segmentation failures produce
output that does not resemble any known Arabic word, and they are fundamentally
harder for a text-only LLM to resolve without access to the document image.

Across PATS-A01 fonts, 71.3\% of OCR error tokens are morphologically valid

wrong word in context. Only 28.7\% of errors produce morphologically invalid

majority of errors are valid-but-wrong.

\textbf{Answer to RQ1:} Qaari produces systematic errors across all nine

dominates PATS-A01 errors (42.1\% of substitutions). The 71.3\%
valid-but-wrong error rate across PATS-A01 establishes the fundamental
limitation of morphological post-processing as a correction strategy.

The results reveal a pronounced font-dependent pattern. The PATS-A01
macro-average CER drops from 14.2\% to 12.3\%, a 13.4\% relative improvement.
However, this aggregate conceals widely varying per-font outcomes.

\textbf{Traditional font: the largest improvement.}
The most striking result is on Traditional font: CER drops from 14.2\% to
2.7\%, an 81.0\% relative reduction. This dramatic improvement suggests that
Qaari's errors on Traditional are predominantly of a type that is
linguistically obvious to the LLM --- likely character substitutions that
produce clearly non-standard word forms that the model confidently corrects.
The Traditional font's specific rendering style appears to trigger a
systematic set of Qaari confusions that the LLM's language prior is well
equipped to resolve.

\textbf{Naskh font: moderate improvement.}
Naskh, the hardest font for Qaari (24.3\% baseline CER), improves by 2.8~pp
to 21.5\% ($-$11.5\% relative). The LLM provides a useful but incomplete
correction: the high error rate means many errors are available to fix, but
the diversity of error patterns on a complex font limits the fraction the
model can resolve.

\textbf{Akhbar font: degradation.}
Akhbar, the easiest font (4.3\% baseline CER), is the only font on which
zero-shot correction increases CER ($+$0.9~pp to 5.2\%). On this font, Qaari
already produces near-correct output; the LLM's tendency to rewrite tokens
--- even with the conservative v2 prompt --- causes net harm. This is the
clearest example of the over-correction problem: a model that is too
interventionist on clean input.

\textbf{Arial, Andalus, Tahoma: marginal improvement.}
These three fonts show small but positive improvements ($-$0.1 to $-$0.2~pp).
Their baseline CER (14.8\%, 15.3\%, 20.6\%) is moderate, and the LLM
improves slightly without making things substantially worse. The marginal
gains suggest the LLM is operating near the break-even point for these fonts.

\textbf{KHATT: marginal improvement despite high baseline.}
On KHATT (44.6\% baseline), zero-shot correction reduces CER by only 1.6~pp
to 43.0\% ($-$3.6\% relative). Despite having the highest baseline error
rate, KHATT benefits less from LLM correction than fonts with equivalent
or lower typewritten CER. The high proportion of segmentation errors
(Section~\ref{subsec:res_p1_taxonomy}) --- which produce unrecognisable
character sequences --- limits the model's ability to determine what was
intended.

when applied to Phase~2 corrections alone, but is tested as a post-processing
layer on the best combination in Phase~6.

Phase~8 uses BM25 character $n$-gram retrieval to inject per-sample few-shot
context from the Phase~2 training corrections. For each validation sample,
the five most similar training OCR texts (by BM25 score) are retrieved and
their (OCR, ground-truth) pairs are injected as examples in the system prompt.

Phase~8 achieves a modest PATS-A01 macro-average improvement of $-$0.2~pp
(12.3\% $\to$ 12.1\%, $-$1.6\% relative over zero-shot). This positions
Phase~8 between Phase~3 ($-$0.3~pp) and Phase~5 ($+$0.1~pp) in terms of
isolated performance. On KHATT, Phase~8 provides a small $-$0.1~pp
improvement (43.0\% $\to$ 42.9\%).

Notably, Phase~8 does not degrade Akhbar (0.0~pp change), unlike Phases~2
and~3. This may reflect the adaptive nature of retrieval: if no sufficiently
similar training sample exists for a very clean input, the model receives
few or no retrieved examples and falls back towards zero-shot behaviour.

The improvement from Phase~8 is modest compared to Phase~4 ($-$0.7~pp) for
two likely reasons. First, BM25 character $n$-gram similarity captures
surface character overlap but may not align with semantic or morphological
correction difficulty. Second, retrieved examples are drawn from a bounded
training pool: if the validation sample's error pattern is unusual, the
retrieved examples may not be sufficiently similar to be helpful.

\textbf{Answer to RQ8:} Same-domain BM25 RAG (Phase~8) provides a modest
improvement of $-$0.2~pp on PATS-A01 and $-$0.1~pp on KHATT over zero-shot.
It outperforms Phase~5 (CAMeL) and does not harm Akhbar, confirming that
domain-matched retrieval avoids the degradation seen with external corpus
RAG (OpenITI). However, it underperforms Phase~3 and Phase~4, suggesting
that targeted global knowledge (confusion matrices, self-reflective insights)
is more effective than per-sample retrieval for this task.
