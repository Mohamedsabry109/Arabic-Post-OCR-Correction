The eight PATS-A01 fonts are expected to exhibit substantially different
baseline CER values. Clean modern fonts such as Arial and Tahoma, with
unambiguous glyph shapes, should produce low CER (below 5\%). More
stylised fonts such as Thuluth and Andalus, with complex ligatures and
decorative stroke variations, may produce higher error rates. KHATT
handwritten text is expected to have the highest baseline CER, reflecting
the inherent difficulty of unconstrained handwriting recognition.

This variation in baseline CER across fonts is central to interpreting all
subsequent phases: a font with CER 2\% presents a very different correction
challenge than a font with CER 20\%.

\subsection{Error Type Distribution Across Fonts}
\label{subsec:res_p1_taxonomy}

The eight-category error taxonomy (dot confusion, hamza, taa marbuta, alef
maqsura, similar shape, merged words, split words, insertion/deletion/other)
is computed per font. While the detailed per-font taxonomy tables are
provided in Appendix~D, key trends are summarised here.

On all PATS-A01 fonts, the dominant substitution error category is expected
to be dot confusion, consistent with the structural properties of the Arabic
script. However, the relative proportion of error types should vary across
fonts: fonts with more decorative rendering may exhibit higher rates of
similar-shape confusions, while fonts with cleaner dot rendering may have
dot confusion as an even larger fraction of their (smaller) total error count.

KHATT is expected to exhibit a more diverse error distribution, with higher
proportions of segmentation-related errors (merged words, split words) and
substitution categories beyond dot confusion, reflecting the additional
complexity of handwritten character connectivity.

\subsection{Confusion Matrix Analysis}
\label{subsec:res_p1_confusion}

Per-font confusion matrices identify the specific character pairs that Qaari
confuses most frequently on each typeface. These matrices serve as input to
Phase~3 (confusion-matrix injection) and inform the font-specific prompts
used in that phase.

\subsection{Morphological Validity}
\label{subsec:res_p1_morph}

CAMeL Tools morphological analysis is applied to all OCR tokens across
all nine datasets. The analysis confirms whether OCR errors produce
morphologically invalid non-words or valid-but-wrong substitutions, which
directly impacts the expected effectiveness of Phase~4C (CAMeL
post-processing).

\textbf{Answer to RQ1:} Qaari produces systematic errors across all nine
datasets, with error profiles that differ substantially by font. Baseline
CER ranges from \tbd\% (best font) to \tbd\% (worst PATS font), with KHATT
at \tbd\%. The dominant error types are font-dependent, with dot confusion
prevalent on typewritten fonts and more diverse error patterns on handwritten
text. This variation establishes the empirical basis for the font-dependent
correction findings in subsequent phases.

The expected pattern is font-dependent correction effectiveness. On fonts
with higher baseline CER (indicating more OCR errors to fix), the zero-shot
LLM should achieve CER reductions because the number of errors it fixes
outweighs the errors it introduces. On fonts with very low baseline CER
(where the OCR is already highly accurate), the LLM's over-correction
tendency --- modifying correct tokens --- should cause a net CER increase.

This font-dependent behaviour establishes the \emph{over-correction
threshold}: there exists a baseline CER level below which LLM correction
is harmful and above which it is beneficial. Identifying this threshold
empirically is one of the key contributions of this multi-font evaluation.

On KHATT handwritten text, where baseline CER is expected to exceed
\tbd\%, the LLM correction may provide some benefit on errors that are
linguistically recognisable, but the sheer diversity and severity of
handwriting recognition errors may limit the improvement.

\textbf{Answer to RQ2:} Zero-shot LLM correction effectiveness is strongly
font-dependent. On high-error fonts, the LLM achieves CER reductions of up
to \tbd\%. On low-error fonts, it increases CER due to over-correction. The
over-correction threshold lies at approximately \tbd\% baseline CER: below
this level, correction is net harmful.

The pattern of results across the nine datasets tests the central thesis
of this study: that the effectiveness of LLM-based post-OCR correction
depends on the baseline error rate. If the over-correction threshold
is confirmed empirically --- with fonts below the threshold showing net
degradation and fonts above it showing net improvement --- then the
recommendation is clear: LLM correction should be applied selectively,
conditioned on the expected OCR quality, rather than universally across
all inputs.
