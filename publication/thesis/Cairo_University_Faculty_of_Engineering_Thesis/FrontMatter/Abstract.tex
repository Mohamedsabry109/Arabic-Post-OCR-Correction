Arabic optical character recognition (OCR) engines produce systematic errors
rooted in the visual properties of the Arabic script: dot-group character
confusions, morphologically valid but contextually wrong substitutions, and
occasional catastrophic repetition artefacts. Open-source engines such as
Qaari fall short of closed-source alternatives, motivating the question of
whether post-OCR correction via a large language model (LLM) can close this
gap without modifying the recognition engine.

This thesis presents a systematic evaluation of prompt-engineering strategies
for Arabic post-OCR correction using Qwen3-4B-Instruct-2507, an open-source
4-billion-parameter instruction-tuned model. Five knowledge augmentation
conditions are evaluated across nine datasets --- eight typewritten fonts from
the PATS-A01 corpus and one handwritten corpus (KHATT) --- each changing
exactly one variable relative to a zero-shot baseline: (1)~character confusion
matrix injection (Phase~3); (2)~self-reflective prompting from training-split
error statistics (Phase~4); and (3)~CAMeL Tools morphological post-processing
with known-overcorrection revert (Phase~5). A combination and ablation study
(Phase~6) tests pre-selected multi-component systems across four configurations:
confusion-matrix only (\texttt{conf\_only}), self-reflective only
(\texttt{self\_only}), both combined (\texttt{conf\_self}), and the best
prompt combination augmented with CAMeL post-processing
(\texttt{best\_camel}).

All eight PATS-A01 fonts share 2,766 text lines drawn from the same underlying
content. An aligned 85/15 train/validation split (seed 42) ensures that every
font receives the same 415 validation samples, so that font is the only
variable when comparing OCR quality and correction effectiveness across
typefaces. KHATT provides a complementary handwritten evaluation with
substantially higher baseline error rates.

Phase~1 establishes the OCR error landscape. Baseline CER on PATS-A01 fonts
ranges from 4.3\% (Akhbar) to 24.3\% (Naskh), with a macro-average of 14.2\%.
KHATT handwritten text reaches 44.6\% CER. The dominant error type across
all typewritten fonts is dot confusion (42\% of substitutions), followed by
similar-shape confusions (18\%) and hamza errors (12\%). Morphological
analysis reveals that 71\% of OCR errors produce valid Arabic words in the
wrong context (\textit{valid-but-wrong} errors), with only 29\% producing
morphologically invalid tokens.

Phase~2 (zero-shot correction) reduces the PATS-A01 macro-average CER from
14.2\% to 12.3\%. However, the effect is strongly font-dependent: on
Traditional font, CER drops from 14.2\% to 2.7\% (an 81.0\% relative
reduction), while on Akhbar (4.3\% baseline), zero-shot correction
\textit{increases} CER to 5.2\%, introducing more errors than it fixes.

Phase~3 (confusion-matrix injection) achieves a PATS-A01 macro-average CER
of 12.0\% ($-$0.3~pp vs.\ Phase~2) and 42.7\% on KHATT ($-$0.3~pp). The
improvement is consistent but modest in isolation.

Phase~4 (self-reflective prompting) achieves the strongest isolated result:
11.6\% on PATS-A01 ($-$0.7~pp vs.\ Phase~2) and 42.8\% on KHATT ($-$0.2~pp).
The self-analysis is derived from Phase~2 training-split failures and includes
both aggregate error statistics and concrete word-level error-correction pairs.

Phase~5 (CAMeL morphological post-processing) produces a slight degradation
($+$0.1~pp on both datasets) due to the preponderance of valid-but-wrong
errors that the morphological filter cannot detect.

Phase~6 combinations demonstrate that Phase~3 and Phase~4 are complementary.
The \texttt{conf\_self} combination achieves 11.2\% CER on PATS-A01 (a
21.1\% relative improvement over the OCR baseline) and 42.4\% on KHATT (a
5.0\% relative improvement). Adding CAMeL post-processing (\texttt{best\_camel})
yields marginal further gain on PATS-A01 (11.1\%) but a slight reversal on
KHATT (42.5\%).

The central finding is that the optimal correction strategy depends on the
baseline error profile of the input. Fonts with CER below approximately 8\%
are harmed by any LLM intervention; fonts in the 8--25\% range benefit
substantially from zero-shot correction, with further gains from knowledge
augmentation. A one-size-fits-all approach to prompt augmentation is unlikely
to succeed across diverse fonts and script types. The thesis contributes an
experimental framework, a three-stage export--infer--analyse pipeline, a
comprehensive multi-dataset evaluation, and an empirical characterisation of
the over-correction threshold that maps the conditions under which LLM-based
post-OCR correction helps, hurts, or has no effect.
