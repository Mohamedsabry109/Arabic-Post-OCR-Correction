Arabic optical character recognition (OCR) engines produce systematic errors
rooted in the visual properties of the Arabic script: dot-group character
confusions, morphologically valid but contextually wrong substitutions, and
segmentation failures that destroy word boundaries. Open-source engines such as
Qaari fall short of closed-source vision--language models, motivating the
question of whether post-OCR correction via a lightweight large language model
(LLM) can bridge this accuracy gap without modifying the recognition engine
and without task-specific fine-tuning.

This thesis presents a controlled, multi-dataset evaluation of
knowledge-augmented prompt-engineering strategies for Arabic post-OCR
correction using Qwen3-4B-Instruct-2507, an open-source 4-billion-parameter
instruction-tuned model. The experimental design follows an isolation
principle: five knowledge augmentation conditions are evaluated across nine
datasets --- eight typewritten fonts from the PATS-A01 corpus (baseline CER
ranging from 4.3\% to 24.3\%) and one handwritten corpus (KHATT, 44.6\%
baseline CER) --- each changing exactly one variable relative to a zero-shot
baseline. The augmentation conditions are: (1)~failure-filtered character
confusion matrix injection, cross-referenced with the model's own zero-shot
failures to retain only actionable confusion pairs (Phase~3);
(2)~self-reflective prompting derived from the model's training-split error
statistics, including quantified fix and introduction rates, concrete
word-level error pairs, and an explicit overcorrection warning (Phase~4);
and (3)~CAMeL Tools morphological post-processing with position-indexed
known-overcorrection revert (Phase~5). A systematic combination and
component-removal ablation study (Phase~6) tests four multi-component
configurations: confusion-matrix only (\texttt{conf\_only}), self-reflective
only (\texttt{self\_only}), both combined (\texttt{conf\_self}), and the best
prompt combination augmented with CAMeL post-processing
(\texttt{best\_camel}).

All eight PATS-A01 fonts share 2,766 text lines drawn from the same underlying
content. An aligned 85/15 train/validation split (seed 42) ensures that every
font receives the same 415 validation samples, so that font is the only
variable when comparing OCR quality and correction effectiveness across
typefaces. KHATT provides a complementary handwritten evaluation with
substantially higher baseline error rates.

Phase~1 establishes the OCR error landscape. Baseline CER on PATS-A01 fonts
ranges from 4.3\% (Akhbar) to 24.3\% (Naskh), with a macro-average of 14.2\%.
KHATT handwritten text reaches 44.6\% CER. The dominant error type across
all typewritten fonts is dot confusion (42\% of substitutions), followed by
similar-shape confusions (18\%) and hamza errors (12\%). Morphological
analysis reveals that 71\% of OCR errors produce valid Arabic words in the
wrong context (\textit{valid-but-wrong} errors), with only 29\% producing
morphologically invalid tokens.

Phase~2 (zero-shot correction) reduces the PATS-A01 macro-average CER from
14.2\% to 12.3\%. However, the effect is strongly font-dependent: on
Traditional font, CER drops from 14.2\% to 2.7\% (an 81.0\% relative
reduction), while on Akhbar (4.3\% baseline), zero-shot correction
\textit{increases} CER to 5.2\%, introducing more errors than it fixes.

Phase~3 (confusion-matrix injection) achieves a PATS-A01 macro-average CER
of 12.0\% ($-$0.3~pp vs.\ Phase~2) and 42.7\% on KHATT ($-$0.3~pp). The
improvement is consistent but modest in isolation.

Phase~4 (self-reflective prompting) achieves the strongest isolated result:
11.6\% on PATS-A01 ($-$0.7~pp vs.\ Phase~2) and 42.8\% on KHATT ($-$0.2~pp).
The self-analysis is derived from Phase~2 training-split failures and includes
both aggregate error statistics and concrete word-level error-correction pairs.

Phase~5 (CAMeL morphological post-processing) produces a slight degradation
($+$0.1~pp on both datasets) due to the preponderance of valid-but-wrong
errors that the morphological filter cannot detect.

Phase~6 combinations demonstrate that Phase~3 and Phase~4 are complementary.
The \texttt{conf\_self} combination achieves 11.2\% CER on PATS-A01 (a
21.1\% relative improvement over the OCR baseline) and 42.4\% on KHATT (a
5.0\% relative improvement). Adding CAMeL post-processing (\texttt{best\_camel})
yields marginal further gain on PATS-A01 (11.1\%) but a slight reversal on
KHATT (42.5\%).

The central finding is the identification and formal characterisation of an
\emph{over-correction threshold}: a baseline CER level ($e^*$) below which the
LLM introduces more errors than it corrects. The threshold arises because the
introduction rate (fraction of correct tokens incorrectly changed) is
approximately constant across fonts, while the fix rate scales with error
density. Empirically, $e^*$ lies at approximately 6--7\% CER for this
model--engine pair: Akhbar (4.3\% baseline) is harmed by every strategy
tested, while Simplified (7.9\%) benefits. Phase~4 is the only strategy that
partially mitigates over-correction on Akhbar, because its diagnostic text
explicitly targets the introduction rate rather than only providing correction
guidance.

The ablation study reveals that Phases~3 and~4 address orthogonal failure
modes---Phase~3 targets the OCR engine's known confusion patterns (raising the
fix rate), while Phase~4 targets the correction model's systematic biases
(lowering the introduction rate)---and combine near-additively. On KHATT
handwritten text, where 37\% of errors are segmentation failures (word merges
and splits that destroy word boundaries), text-only correction is structurally
limited regardless of prompt strategy, achieving only 3.6--5.0\% relative
improvement despite a 44.6\% baseline CER.

This thesis contributes: (1)~a formal over-correction threshold model with
empirical estimation; (2)~a novel self-reflective prompting strategy for OCR
correction achieving the strongest isolated result; (3)~a proof of
complementarity between confusion-matrix and self-reflective augmentation;
(4)~a comprehensive nine-dataset evaluation under aligned splits isolating
font as the sole variable; (5)~a three-stage export--infer--analyse pipeline
enabling GPU-free local reproducibility; and (6)~an empirical characterisation
of the structural limits of text-only correction on handwritten text.
