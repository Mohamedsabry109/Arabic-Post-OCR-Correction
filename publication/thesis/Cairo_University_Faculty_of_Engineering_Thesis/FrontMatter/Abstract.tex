Arabic optical character recognition (OCR) engines produce systematic errors
rooted in the visual properties of the Arabic script: dot-group character
confusions, morphologically valid but contextually wrong substitutions, and
occasional catastrophic repetition artefacts. Open-source engines such as
Qaari fall short of closed-source alternatives, motivating the question of
whether post-OCR correction via a large language model (LLM) can close this
gap without modifying the recognition engine.

This thesis presents a systematic evaluation of prompt-engineering strategies
for Arabic post-OCR correction using Qwen3-4B-Instruct-2507, an open-source
4-billion-parameter instruction-tuned model. Six knowledge augmentation
conditions are evaluated in isolation on 200 samples of the PATS-A01 Akhbar
typewritten corpus, each changing exactly one variable relative to a zero-shot
baseline: (1) character confusion matrix injection (Phase~3); (2) Arabic
orthographic rule augmentation (Phase~4A); (3) few-shot examples from the
QALB corpus (Phase~4B); (4) CAMeL Tools morphological post-processing
(Phase~4C); (5) self-reflective prompting from training-split error statistics
(Phase~4D); and (6) retrieval-augmented generation with the OpenITI classical
corpus (Phase~5). A combination and ablation study (Phase~6) tests twelve
pre-selected multi-component systems.

The Qaari baseline achieves 1.37\% character error rate (CER) on normal
samples and 17.01\% overall, with the discrepancy caused by a repetition
artefact in 0.5\% of samples. Zero-shot LLM correction (Phase~2) reduces
aggregate CER to 5.77\% (66\% relative reduction) by collapsing repetition
artefacts, but raises CER on normal samples from 1.37\% to 5.77\%, revealing
a systematic over-correction problem: the model modifies correct tokens at a
25.06\% error introduction rate. Every knowledge augmentation strategy
tested degrades performance relative to the zero-shot baseline, with
degradations ranging from 75\% (Phase~4B few-shot) to 283\% (Phase~5 RAG).
CAMeL post-processing has no effect, as all OCR errors and LLM corrections
are morphologically valid Arabic forms and the revert condition is never
triggered.

The central finding is that, on high-quality typewritten Arabic input, more
prompt information consistently worsens correction because each additional
knowledge element activates the model's intervention tendency, increasing
the rate at which correct tokens are corrupted. The simplest strategy ---
zero-shot correction applied selectively to high-error samples --- is both
the most effective and the most practical recommendation. Future work should
evaluate on handwritten text (KHATT), replace QALB with OCR-native few-shot
pairs, and build domain-matched retrieval corpora to test whether these
findings generalise to higher-error-rate and domain-aligned settings.
