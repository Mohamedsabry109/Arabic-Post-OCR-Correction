\ThesisTitle{Knowledge-Augmented Large Language Models for Arabic Post-OCR Error Correction}

\ThesisKeywords{Arabic OCR; Post-OCR Correction; Large Language Models; Prompt Engineering; Qwen3; PATS-A01; KHATT; Error Taxonomy; Self-Reflective Prompting; CAMeL Tools}

\ThesisSummary{This thesis investigates whether a lightweight open-source instruction-tuned
large language model (Qwen3-4B-Instruct-2507, 4 billion parameters) can correct systematic
errors produced by the Qaari Arabic OCR engine through prompt engineering alone, without
task-specific fine-tuning. Five knowledge-augmentation strategies are evaluated in a
controlled isolation design across nine datasets---eight typewritten fonts from PATS-A01
(4.3--24.3\% baseline CER) and one handwritten corpus from KHATT (44.6\% baseline CER)---each
changing exactly one variable relative to a zero-shot baseline. Strategies include
confusion-matrix injection filtered by LLM failures (Phase~3), self-reflective prompting
derived from the model's own training-split error statistics (Phase~4), morphological
post-processing via CAMeL Tools with known-overcorrection revert (Phase~5), and systematic
strategy combinations with component-removal ablation (Phase~6). The central finding is the
identification and formal characterisation of an \emph{over-correction threshold}: a baseline
CER below which the LLM introduces more errors than it fixes ($e^* \approx$ 6--7\% for
this model--engine pair). Above this threshold, the best combination---confusion-matrix
injection paired with self-reflective prompting (\texttt{conf\_self})---achieves 11.2\%
CER on PATS-A01 (a 21.1\% relative reduction vs.\ OCR baseline) and 42.4\% on KHATT
(5.0\% relative reduction), with statistical significance on six of nine datasets at
Bonferroni-corrected $\alpha = 0.0125$. Ablation analysis reveals that the two strategies
address orthogonal failure modes and combine near-additively, with self-reflective prompting
contributing approximately twice the marginal improvement of confusion-matrix injection.
On handwritten text, where 37\% of errors are segmentation failures inaccessible to
text-only correction, improvement is structurally limited---motivating future multi-modal
approaches. The thesis contributes an experimental framework, a three-stage
export--infer--analyse pipeline, and an empirical characterisation of the conditions under
which LLM-based post-OCR correction helps, hurts, or has no effect.}
